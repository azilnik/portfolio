# Portfolio POV 2026

A curated reference library of what hiring managers at premium product companies
(Wealthsimple, Shopify, Stripe, Notion, Linear, Vercel, Cash App, Perplexity, Lovable)
are publicly saying about portfolios, craft, AI fluency, and player-coach design roles
in 2024–2026.

Every claim is sourced with a direct URL. No secondary synthesis, no generic "experts
agree" framing. Use this to guide rewrites of Ari's three featured case studies.

**How to read this file:** Each section opens with the primary evidence (quotes +
attributions), then ends with a short takeaway. The final "Synthesis for Ari's portfolio"
section distills everything into 10 actionable principles.

## 1. Standing panelists

### 1a. Polly D'Arcy — VP of Design, Wealthsimple

Polly has been at Wealthsimple since Sept 2019 and was promoted to VP of Design in
Feb 2025 (per her LinkedIn). She owns design across investing, banking, and growth
for Canada's largest fintech (~$100B AUM, 4M+ users). Notably, she does **not** appear
on the major design podcast circuits (Dive Club, Design Better, Lenny's), so her public
POV lives primarily in her LinkedIn hiring posts, her product-launch stage appearances,
and the language of the Wealthsimple culture manual + her own team's job posts (which
she or her directs oversee).

> https://ca.linkedin.com/in/pollydarcy

**Synthesis — what a portfolio needs to resonate with Polly:**

1. **Craft + consumer fintech taste.** She repeats "strong craft" and "loves consumer
   products" in every hiring post. Visual polish is not optional; it's the opening filter.
2. **Strategy at the front.** Every Wealthsimple job post leads with "design is a
   strategic lever — we don't just make things beautiful, we help shape what gets built."
   Case studies must show the designer shaping what got built, not just styling it.
3. **Ambiguous → clarity.** The portfolio bar they publish literally says: "how you've
   tackled ambiguous product challenges and found clarity." This maps perfectly to
   Ari's "thrives in ambiguity / 0-to-1" positioning.
4. **Business impact + client advocacy, paired.** The job post asks for both "your
   impact on the product and business" **and** "how you've used insights to advocate
   for client needs." Numbers alone won't do it; neither will research theater.
5. **Bias toward action / iterative shipping.** The Wealthsimple culture manual's
   "Ship It, then improve it" is aggressive. A case study that reads like it took nine
   months of perfect process will signal culture misfit. Show iteration and post-launch
   learning.
6. **AI fluency as "natural extension of how you solve problems."** Wealthsimple's own
   hiring essay explicitly rejects AI-as-novelty. Case studies should show AI tools
   embedded in the workflow (prototyping, research synthesis, code-generation), not
   bolted on as a sidebar.

> — Carl Rivera, Double Diamond NYC recap, Feb 19 2026,

**On what he scans for in portfolios — craft first, always:**

> — Carl Rivera, framing question for the Dive Club episode, Sep 19 2025,
> https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YcYH5v5BPoY

> "Basically, like exercise the right that we were hired for — our taste, our point of
> view. And we get to say, we get to decide what good looks like."
> — Carl Rivera, Dive Club transcript opening,
> https://podscan.fm/podcasts/dive-club/episodes/carl-rivera-shopifys-big-bet-on-design-and-craft-as-the-differentiator

**On designers shipping code (directly relevant to Ari's framing):**

> "As part of onboarding, every designer makes a pull request into production. It's
> not about forcing designers to code — it's about pushing past fear. Once you do, it
> becomes natural."
> — Carl Rivera, quoted in Alex Pavlou LinkedIn post, March 2 2026,
> https://www.linkedin.com/posts/alexpavlou_it-all-started-with-a-random-meeting-a-few-activity-7434244301548756995-q9Ys

> "Fear combined with startup cost. You open up cursor or you open up terminal and
> they really don't feel like welcoming interfaces. […] Once you get past it, it's
> very natural and fluid and very easy."
> — Carl Rivera on designer/code fluency, Double Diamond NYC,
> https://doublediamondnyc.substack.com/p/carl-rivera-chief-design-officer

> "Over 50% of 170+ designers actively merge code, with 1,800 pull requests in six
> months."
> — Double Diamond NYC reporting on Shopify's design org, Feb 19 2026,
> https://doublediamondnyc.substack.com/p/carl-rivera-chief-design-officer

**On leadership through action:**

> "Be a leader. You can claim that space and you can be a leader well before you have
> people report into you in an org chart. […] If any person cares about it, then just
> start to speak about it and own that space in the conversation and have the most
> ideas and be the most passionate about your ideas and fight for your ideas."
> — Carl Rivera, Double Diamond NYC,
> https://doublediamondnyc.substack.com/p/carl-rivera-chief-design-officer

**Synthesis — what Rivera wants a portfolio to show:**

1. **Craft is the first filter, no matter the seniority.** He explicitly says he
   scans portfolio examples and if the aesthetic craft isn't there, the rest doesn't
   matter.
2. **A spiky, stated point of view.** He prefers candidates who rate 1 or 4 on the
   interview panel (i.e., polarizing) over safe 3s. A portfolio that reads like it
   was written to offend no one will bore him.
3. **AI-era craft thesis.** "When everyone can generate a 7/10," the portfolio's job
   is to show the 10s — the opinionated choices that wouldn't have come from a prompt.
4. **Code / technical fluency is table stakes at Shopify.** Not "I'm full-stack" but
   "I'm not afraid of the terminal and I ship when I need to." Ari's framing of
   "AI-coded prototypes to derisk library selection" lands here.
5. **Ownership without title.** His strongest advice to aspiring leaders is to claim
   leadership through passion and conviction before the org chart grants it. Case
   studies should show that behavior: stepping past role boundaries when the work needs it.

### 2a. Katie Dill — Head of Design, Stripe

Katie has been Head of Design at Stripe since March 2021. Primary sources:
- Dive Club, Feb 23 2026 — "The new era of design at Stripe" (49 min):
  https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Dpy-yyYXhgU
- Lenny's Podcast, Oct 15 2023 — "Building beautiful products":
  https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=gfEEcssu304
- The General Podcast with Randy Hunt, Jul 2025:
  https://www.thegp.com/news/on-design-leadership-with-katie-dill-stripe-and-randy-hunt-notion

**On what she looks for in designers (2026 evolution):**

> "Certainly curiosity always would have been something that I would probably have
> shared with you on my top list. You know, I'm looking for people that are humble,
> have exquisite talent, and that they are curious and that they're going to really
> just be constantly looking to grow and constantly better understand the circumstances
> of the work that they're doing and their users' experience or their collaborators."
> — Katie Dill, Dive Club, Feb 23 2026,
> https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Dpy-yyYXhgU

> "What does that curiosity look like today has evolved. […] Everything — you think
> you're on top of it one day and then tomorrow there's a new model and a new tool
> and a new possibility. I don't expect every person we meet to have it figured out
> […] but that they demonstrate a genuine interest."
> — Katie Dill, Dive Club, Feb 23 2026,
> https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Dpy-yyYXhgU

**On hiring — humility, chutzpah, hustle:**

> "When hiring a designer, it's easier to teach tools and process than it is [to teach]
> taste and character. Therefore, look for someone with great judgment, taste, and
> humility. […] That courage inside them to fight for great is pretty important. And
> that hustle to try to execute on that rapidly is, of course, essential."
> — Katie Dill, Lenny's Podcast summary,
> https://podpulse.ai/podcast-notes-and-takeaways/lennys-podcast-product-growth-career-building-beautiful-products-with-stripes-head-of-design-katie-dill-stripe-airbnb-lyft

**On quality as a group effort (a direct rebuke to solo-hero portfolio narratives):**

> "Quality is definitely a group effort. You're sunk if you think that you can just
> hire some incredibly talented person and they'll do it, that'll be fine. […] What
> you communicate inside will then eventually show up outside."
> — Katie Dill, Lenny's Podcast,
> https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=gfEEcssu304

**On "fewer memos, more demos" (Stripe's prototyping philosophy):**

> "The case for 'fewer memos, more demos' and how Stripe operationalizes quality."
> — The General Podcast summary, Jul 2025,
> https://www.thegp.com/news/on-design-leadership-with-katie-dill-stripe-and-randy-hunt-notion

**Takeaway:** Dill's portfolio bar prioritizes judgment, taste, humility, and hustle
over process theater. "Exquisite talent" and "courage to fight for great" are her words,
not resume-speak. Ari's portfolio should lead with demos and prototypes, not memos —
and should honestly acknowledge the team around each win, not overclaim solo impact.

### 2b. Hannah Hearth — Head of Product Design, Vercel

Hannah joined Vercel as Head of Product Design in late 2025. Primary sources:
- Dive Club, Feb 9 2026 — "Design Careers in the Age of AI":
  https://dive-club.beehiiv.com/p/hunting-for-curiosity
- Her own essays at hannahhearth.com (she writes publicly and is notably candid).

**On portfolios (2026) — the cookie-cutter case study is dead:**

> "I am so glad we are past this era where every case study was like 'here's how I
> wrote a sticky note.'"
> — Hannah Hearth, Dive Club Feb 9 2026,
> https://dive-club.beehiiv.com/p/hunting-for-curiosity

> "Much more so than two years ago, showcasing in your portfolio that you tried
> something new lately and you used a new tool or a new process — it's like table
> stakes at this point."
> — Hannah Hearth, Dive Club Feb 9 2026,
> https://dive-club.beehiiv.com/p/hunting-for-curiosity

**On her #1 trait for great designers:**

> "[The #1 trait is] sharing work early and often. […] As long as you're sharing the
> right context, you should be getting feedback as early and as often as you can,
> because the more feedback you get, the more you're going to learn quickly."
> — Hannah Hearth, Dive Club Feb 9 2026,
> https://dive-club.beehiiv.com/p/hunting-for-curiosity

**On design leadership shipping code themselves:**

> "[Vercel's designers] are significantly more successful in getting their work shipped
> by being part of the shipping process. […] Vercel's designers regularly use Claude
> Code and Cursor. Hannah even shipped a PR in her first week on the job!"
> — Dive Club takeaways, Feb 9 2026,
> https://dive-club.beehiiv.com/p/hunting-for-curiosity

**On Vercel's design leadership bar (take-home for a Head of Design role):**

> "When interviewing at Vercel, Hannah had to do a take-home assignment for the first
> time in 10 years. Gone are the days of pure people managers who just hire smart
> people and get out of the way. Hannah says we've entered into the 'new era of
> design leadership.'"
> — Dive Club takeaways, Feb 9 2026,
> https://dive-club.beehiiv.com/p/hunting-for-curiosity

**On design manager portfolios (her own essay, pre-Vercel but foundational):**

> "When selecting case studies, make sure the scope and complexity match the role
> you're interviewing for. Do they showcase projects that span beyond a single team,
> or single area of the product? Be explicitly clear to your audience why you selected
> each case study—tell them what you want them to take away from it."
> — Hannah Hearth, "The sprawl of design manager portfolios", Sep 17 2024,
> https://www.hannahhearth.com/posts/the-sprawl-of-design-manager-portfolios

> "Consider splitting up your presentation into two primary case studies that help
> showcase both your breadth and your depth. For example: One case study could lean
> heavily on high level design strategy. […] This leaves room for a second case study
> that leans more heavily on tactical execution."
> — Hannah Hearth, same essay,
> https://www.hannahhearth.com/posts/the-sprawl-of-design-manager-portfolios

**Takeaway:** Hannah wants to see curiosity made visible — evidence you tried a new
tool, process, or side project recently — plus case studies where you explicitly
state why you chose them. For player-coach candidates, she recommends splitting into
"depth" + "breadth" case studies. Ari's three-case-study structure already maps to this.

### 2f. Randy Hunt — Head of Design, Notion

### Tom Scott on what "great" looks like at IC level in 2026

> "For ICs: three to five focused case studies, no more. Each should reflect the type

> https://verifiedinsider.substack.com/p/faq-design-hiring-in-2026

> "Show your most recent work, not your most polished old work. The demand for

**Takeaway for structure:**
- **3 deep case studies**, not 5–8. (Ari's portfolio strategy already matches.)

- **Show pivots and messy middle**, not a perfect-process narrative. 78% of managers

- **Recency matters.** Case studies older than ~2 years need to earn their place.
- **Visual craft is non-negotiable**, even for UX-heavy case studies.

## Synthesis for Ari's portfolio

These are the 10 concrete, actionable principles the portfolio agents should apply when
rewriting case studies. Each maps back to sources above.

### 1. Open every case study with a business-outcome headline, not a project name
The first thing on every case study must be a metric + business impact + timeframe in
the first paragraph — pyramid principle. Not "Join Ideas: An AI Materials Marketplace"
but something like "$500K in construction savings in 30 days: how an AI research pivot
saved a materials marketplace." (Dill, Rivera, Hearth, UXfolio 74-recruiter survey,
Minto pyramid.)

### 2. Craft is the opening filter. Every screen must look premium.
Visual polish is ranked #1 by 58% of hiring managers in 2026 (Figma). Rivera, Worboys,
Saarinen, and Tom Scott all say the same thing in different words: **if the craft isn't
there on first scan, the rest of the story never gets read.** Before the words land,
the typography, spacing, imagery, motion, and full-bleed visuals have to feel senior.
Polly D'Arcy's hiring posts repeat "strong craft" every single time.

### 3. Show the messy middle and the scars. No flawless hero narratives.
78% of hiring managers are skeptical of flawless case studies (UX Hiring Trends via
Borade Solanki). Ari's CLAUDE.md already warns against "robotic positivity" — amplify
this. Every case study should acknowledge a pivot, a thing that didn't work, a tradeoff,
or a decision not to build something. Rivera, Hearth, and Dill all prefer candidates
who can reason about constraints and admit what they chose not to do.

### 4. Show one prototype or motion artifact per case study
Static-only portfolios are read as dated in 2026. A 20-second prototype recording beats
20 static screens (Bölter, 150-portfolio review). Every case study needs at least one:
a short video walkthrough, an animated GIF of an interaction, a full-bleed autoplay
demo, or an interactive prototype link. (This matches Ari's existing `VideoPlayer`,
`FullBleedGif`, and `FullBleedImage` components.)

### 5. AI fluency must be woven into the workflow, not a sidebar
"AI is table stakes" is now repeated by every source in the Dive Club / Config tier
(Rivera, Worboys, Hearth, Dill, Wealthsimple CPO). Each case study should show at
least one specific, *named* way AI shaped the work: prototype derisking, research
synthesis, code-generated demos, library validation, rapid iteration. Avoid the
"resume-stuffer" pattern — never list AI tools without showing the artifact or decision
they enabled. (Builder.io, Tom Scott, Wealthsimple hiring essay.)

### 6. Frame code contributions as AI-coded prototyping for decision-making, not "full-stack designer"
This is already in Ari's CLAUDE.md writing guidelines — keep it. Rivera's framing is
the gold standard: designers ship code not to become engineers, but to push past fear
and to reason about engineering constraints directly. Phrase Ari's contributions as
"I build AI-coded prototypes to derisk library selection and validate technical
feasibility before engineering commits resources," as CLAUDE.md already prescribes.

### 7. Be explicit about the *you* vs. the team
Matej Latin's "I'm hiring you, not your team" rule is still repeated across 2024–2026
hiring essays. Use first-person active voice throughout, but when the work was a team
effort, name the team and be specific about your contribution ("Led interaction design,
partnered with engineering, paired with research on the methodology"). Dill's "quality
is a group effort" quote means you must show collaborative credit *without* hiding
your own leverage point.

### 8. Curate aggressively. Three deep case studies is the ceiling, not the floor
Recruiters explicitly say "one project explained really well beats five explained
quickly" (UXfolio 74-recruiter survey; Borade Solanki's 1-in-4 response rate after
cutting from 9 to 3). Ari's strategy of exactly 3 deep case studies + a side-projects
section is already aligned — commit to it fully. Do not let any of the 3 dilute each
other. Every case study must pull its weight on a different story arc (Join = AI
fluency + research rigor + business impact; Blockdaemon = 0-to-1 leadership + scaling;
ha-smart-hood-vent = systems thinking + builder mindset).

### 9. Lead with strategic framing — "design shaped what got built"
Polly D'Arcy's team tagline is "Design is a strategic lever at Wealthsimple — we don't
just make things beautiful, we help shape what gets built." This is the exact framing
senior/staff portfolios need to earn. Every case study should have a section where it's
clear the designer changed the *what*, not just the *how*. Hearth's "design management
depth + breadth" framework is useful here: make sure the two non-AI case studies have
visible strategy (influence on roadmap, team development, scope decisions) alongside
tactical craft.
