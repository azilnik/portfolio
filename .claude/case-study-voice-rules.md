# Case Study Voice Rules

_Established April 2026. Derived from a line-by-line analysis of the
strongest portfolios in the competitive set (Ainara Sainz, Tyler
Kotsopoulos, Danny Williams) vs. all 7 ari.design case studies._

---

## The core problem these rules fix

The current case studies read like someone justifying their seniority
to a skeptical audience. They over-explain decisions, qualify every
metric, narrate the internal politics of every pivot, and insert
meta-commentary about process. The competitive set does the opposite:
it shows the product, states the outcome, and moves on. Confidence
is silence, not explanation.

---

## Three exemplary references

Every rule below cites specific behavior from these three pieces.

### 1. Ainara Sainz — Premium+ (Wealthsimple)
https://ainarasainz.com/work/premium

~500 words. Product-led. Uses "we" naturally. Describes what the
product does, not what the designer decided. Results stated in one
line each. Team credits at the bottom with emoji. Zero process
narration. Zero self-justification. Zero "what I chose not to build."

**Why it works:** A hiring manager reads this and thinks "she shipped
a real feature that users love." They never think about her process
because the product IS the argument.

### 2. Ainara Sainz — NBA TopShot (Dapper Labs)
https://ainarasainz.com/work/nba-topshot

Handles massive results ($700M, 1.1M users, $230K single sale)
without over-explaining. External validation is dropped casually:
"Collins Dictionary called 'NFT' the 2021 word of the year." Credits
name every collaborator by role. The tone is proud without performing
pride.

**Why it works:** The results speak. She doesn't explain how she
measured them or caveat the methodology. The external validation
(Collins Dictionary, the LeBron sale price) does more for credibility
than any paragraph about research rigor.

### 3. Tyler Kotsopoulos — Capital One Travel (Hopper)
https://www.tylerkotsopoulos.ca/capitalonetravel

~200 words of his own copy. Five press quotes from CNBC, The Points
Guy, Fast Company, Forbes, and Business Insider. "Further details
about this project are confidential." That's it. Maximum confidence.
The world validates the work; he doesn't need to.

**Why it works:** When CNBC says your product is exciting, you don't
need a "How the project ran" section. Tyler understood that the case
study's job is to prove the work mattered, and press quotes do that
better than any first-person narration.

## The 12 rules

### 1. Show the product, not the process

Describe what the user sees and does. Don't describe what you
researched, debated, or decided unless the decision IS the product
story.

- **Ainara (Premium+):** "Perks are grouped into clear categories,
  making benefits easy to understand, even with new brands." — This
  describes the product. Not "I decided to group perks into categories
  because research showed..."
- **Current ari.design:** "Three leaders had bought into the
  marketplace direction, and engineering was already scoped against it.
  I made the case to the VP of Partnerships first because her scope was
  most affected." — This is backstage narration. Cut it.

### 2. Cut to 500-800 words

The competitive set runs 200-500 words per case study. Ari's run
1,600-2,600. Aim for 500-800: enough to tell the story, short enough
to respect a hiring manager's time.

- **Ainara (Crypto):** ~400 words for a year-long project with
  multiple features.
- **Current ari.design (Blockdaemon Staking):** ~2,600 words for a
  similar scope. Cut by 70%.

### 3. Kill the rigid template

No more Problem → Insight → PivotCard → Solution → "What I chose
not to build" → Outcome → "How the project ran" → Lessons →
Reflection. This template makes every case study feel identical and
performative.

Let each story find its own shape. Ainara's Premium+ has: intro →
feature descriptions → quote → results → credits. Her NBA TopShot
has: intro → product descriptions → results → credits. Different
shapes for different stories.

### 4. Delete self-justification sections

Remove all "What I chose not to build" sections. Remove all "How the
project ran" sections. Remove all `<Lessons>` components. Remove all
`<details>` reflections.

These sections exist to prove maturity. They prove anxiety. A hiring
manager who reads a clear product story with strong results already
knows you're senior. You don't need to tell them.

- **Tyler (Capital One):** Has none of these. Is a Principal Product
  Designer at Hopper.
- **Ainara (Premium+):** Has none of these. Is Staff Product Designer
  at Wealthsimple.

### 5. Use "we" more than "I"

Ainara writes "we designed," "we launched," "we released." This reads
as confident leadership. Ari writes "I made the case," "I pushed,"
"I built." This reads as anxious credit-claiming.

"We" signals you led a team. "I" signals you're worried nobody will
know you did the work. Secure leaders use "we."

Exception: "I" is fine for clearly individual actions. "I joined as
the founding designer." "I grew the team from 1 to 7."

### 6. Let metrics breathe

State the metric. Don't explain the measurement methodology. Don't
qualify it. Don't compare it to other case studies' metrics.

- **Ainara (TopShot):** "We reached 1.1 million registered users. We
  sold over $700 million in Top Shot Moments." Period.
- **Current ari.design:** "The 50% measurement here is specific and
  separate from Join Ideas' 50% number; Ideas measured material-cost
  savings via the estimate tool, and Scenarios measures sign-off cycle
  time via product analytics." — Nobody asked for disambiguation.

If a metric needs a caveat, one sentence. "Exact numbers can't be
shared due to company policy" (Ainara). Not a paragraph.

### 7. Credits replace team paragraphs

A Credits section at the bottom (name + role) replaces all paragraphs
about team structure, org size, who reported to whom, and how many
contractors you had. Ainara does this on every case study. It signals
leadership and collaboration without narrating it.

### 8. No performed vulnerability

Remove "The blunt truth," "That scar shows up," "the honest caveat,"
"I'm not comfortable defending the multiplier." These phrases perform
self-awareness instead of demonstrating it.

Actual vulnerability is Ainara writing "Exact numbers can't be shared
due to company policy" — a real constraint stated plainly. Not "The
blunt truth about V1 is that it shipped without a formal research
practice behind it. That scar shows up in the Reflection below.
It's a real one."

### 9. Don't narrate stakeholder politics

"I made the case to the VP of Partnerships first because her scope
was most affected. Once she moved, the CEO and Head of Product
Development followed." — This is interesting to Ari. It is not
interesting to a hiring manager scanning 15 portfolios.

State the outcome: "Research shifted the direction from a marketplace
to a recommendation engine." Who you convinced, in what order, is
backstage.

### 10. Don't force-insert AI fluency

"The AI fluency angle on this project was visual density validation"
reads as checking a box. If AI is genuinely part of the story (Join
Ideas is an AI product), it belongs. If you're grafting an AI angle
onto a navigation redesign, cut it.

### 11. One voice, varied shapes

Every case study should feel like the same person wrote it. But the
structures should vary. A side project reads differently from an
enterprise case study. A founding-designer story reads differently
from a team-lead story. Don't force them into one mold.

### 12. External validation > self-reported metrics

One press quote or customer quote does more for credibility than
a paragraph of self-reported metrics with methodology caveats.
Tyler's entire Capital One case study is proof.

Drop external validation casually, not defensively.
- "Join is used by 1,000+ companies including DPR Construction."
- Blockdaemon raised $207M at $3.25B.
- A customer saved $500K in their first 30 days.

## What to keep

The existing case studies have genuine strengths that should survive
the rewrite:

- **Business-outcome-led titles.** These are better than the
  competitive set.
- **The PivotCard component.** The pivot-moment callout is a strong
  structural element. Keep it but use it once per study, not as a
  mandatory template section.
- **Customer quotes.** The Join case studies have real customer quotes.
  Keep them. They're the closest thing to external validation.
- **The "like X for Y" framing.** "Like AWS for crypto" and "like Jira
  for construction" are immediately useful context.
- **The hood vent case study's personality.** "I have two small kids.
  I read too many air quality studies." This is the right kind of
  personal voice.

---

## Checklist for every case study before ship

- [ ] Under 800 words (body text, not counting component props)?
- [ ] No "What I chose not to build" section?
- [ ] No "How the project ran" section?
- [ ] No `<Lessons>` component?
- [ ] No `<details>` reflection?
- [ ] No stakeholder-politics narration?
- [ ] No measurement-methodology explanation?
- [ ] No performed vulnerability language?
- [ ] Credits section at the bottom?
- [ ] "We" used more than "I" (except for clearly individual actions)?
- [ ] Reads in under 3 minutes?
