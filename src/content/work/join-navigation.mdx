Navigation isn't a menu. It's the product's assertion about how work is organized. When the nav matches the user's mental model, everything feels obvious. When it doesn't, users can't describe where they are, can't predict where things live, and every session starts with reorientation. A nav redesign isn't a UI project. It's a structural claim about the domain.

## The evidence

Join is like Jira for construction. The old navigation had grown feature-by-feature, with labels that drifted away from how users described their work. Customer success was absorbing tickets clustered around "how do I get to X." Usability sessions confirmed the gap: users couldn't describe Join's structure in the product's own terms. They described it in terms of projects.

So we rebuilt around projects. Portfolio at the top. Project as the primary container. Features as sub-navigation inside the project's context.

## The craft

**A two-click zoom rhythm.** From the portfolio overview, two clicks take you into any view inside a project. From deep inside a project, two clicks take you back out. Symmetric. The rhythm is the part users feel, even if they can't articulate why it's easier. Predictability compounds — every session reinforces the spatial model.

**Feature discovery as a side effect.** When users land on a project, the nav surfaces features relevant to that project's current phase. Discovery becomes natural, not something the product has to teach. The structure does what onboarding tours try to do, but permanently and in context.

## The numbers

Support tickets on navigation questions dropped 32% after launch. Users started reaching for the org-level portfolio views in ways they hadn't before. The zoom-out had existed in the old nav, technically, but nobody used it because there wasn't a clear path back in.

## The takeaway

When users struggle with navigation, the instinct is to add labels, tooltips, onboarding flows. Those are patches on a structural problem. The fix is making the nav match the mental model the user already has. If construction teams think in projects, the nav is projects. Everything else follows.
