Join is like Jira for construction. As Head of Design, I lead a small team and ship product across the platform.

The new navigation rebuilds Join's structure around projects. The old nav had grown feature-by-feature, with labels that drifted away from how users described their work. Customer success was fielding "how do I get to X" tickets. Usability sessions confirmed users couldn't describe Join's structure in the product's own terms. They described it in terms of projects.

Rebuilding around projects collapsed the path between any feature and the portfolio overview to two clicks.

## Projects as the top-level unit

The nav is the portfolio, then the project you're working in. Features sit under the project, sequenced by phase — preconstruction features when the project's preconstruction, change-management features when it's in build.

## A two-click zoom rhythm

Two clicks down from the portfolio to any view inside a project, two clicks back out. The path is the same length in both directions, which is the part users notice.

## Feature discovery as a side effect

When a project manager opens a project mid-build, the nav puts change orders and RFIs at the top, not buried under preconstruction tools they used six months ago. People find features by being in the work that uses them, not by hunting through a menu.

## Results

Support tickets on navigation questions dropped 32% after launch. Users started reaching for the org-level portfolio views in ways they hadn't before. The zoom-out had existed in the old nav, technically, but nobody used it because there wasn't a clear path back in.
