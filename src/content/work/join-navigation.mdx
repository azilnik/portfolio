## The setup

Join is like Jira for construction. I'm Head of Design, leading a small team that ships across the platform.

The navigation problem had been accumulating for years. Every time Join shipped a new feature, someone added a nav item. Reasonable decision each time. Completely incoherent result. The sidebar had grown into a flat list of features that didn't map to anything in a user's mental model. Customer success was absorbing a steady stream of tickets clustered around a single theme: "how do I get to X?"

We ran usability sessions and the signal was damning. Users couldn't describe Join's structure using the product's own vocabulary. They didn't say "I go to the Cost Report module." They said "I go to my project and look at costs." Everything was project-first in their heads. The nav was feature-first on screen. The question was whether we could rebuild around projects without breaking the muscle memory of existing power users.

## What we tried

The obvious first attempt was relabeling. Take the existing structure, rename things to match user language, ship it in a week. Mark and I mapped the old labels against user interview language and the gaps were too wide. It wasn't a naming problem. It was a structural one. Users thought in terms of "my project, then the work I'm doing in it." The nav was organized as "the feature, then which project."

Relabeling a bad hierarchy makes it a well-labeled bad hierarchy.

## What actually worked

We rebuilt the navigation around projects as the top-level unit instead of a flat list of features. From the user's perspective the structure now matches the way they describe their work: portfolio first, then the specific project you're inside, then the features available within that project — sequenced to match the phase of work in front of them.

The piece users actually feel is the symmetry of getting around — every deep view inside a project is two clicks from the portfolio overview, and the portfolio is two clicks back from any deep view. Once that pattern is in place, estimators stop asking where things are; they just go.

## The details that mattered

**Feature discovery as a side effect.** When users land on a project, the nav surfaces features relevant to that project's current phase. This was the sneaky benefit of project-first architecture. In the old nav, users had to know a feature existed to find it. In the new nav, landing on a project during the estimating phase naturally surfaces the estimating tools alongside the ones you came looking for. Discovery becomes passive instead of taught.

## Where it landed

Support tickets on navigation questions dropped 32% after launch, but the more interesting shift was behavioral — users started reaching for the org-level portfolio views in ways they hadn't before. Technically the zoom-out had existed in the old nav, but nobody used it because there was no clear path back in. Once people had confidence they could return, they started exploring.

One customer called the team "very brave" for tackling it. Which is either a compliment or a way of saying they expected it to go badly. Either way, it didn't.
