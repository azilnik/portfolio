When users can't describe your product in your product's own terms, the information architecture is wrong. Not the users. Join's navigation had drifted feature-by-feature until the labels on screen didn't match the words customers used to describe their work. They described it in terms of projects.

## The situation

Join is like Jira for construction. I lead a small design team and ship product across the platform.

The nav had grown organically. Every shipped feature got its own top-level entry. The result was a flat list of capabilities that made sense to the team that built them and confused everyone else. Customer success was absorbing support tickets clustered around "how do I get to X." Usability sessions confirmed the gap: users couldn't map Join's structure to their mental model. They thought in projects. The nav thought in features.

## The wrong direction

The first instinct was to clean up labels and reorder the existing structure. Better names, fewer items, maybe a collapsible section or two. It's the lowest-risk move. It's also cosmetic surgery on a structural problem. Renaming items in a feature-centric nav doesn't change the fact that the nav is feature-centric. Users would still land on a list of tools when they were looking for a project.

## The bet

Rebuild the entire navigation around projects. The portfolio is the top level. A project is the second level. Features show up as sub-navigation inside the project context, sequenced to match the phase of work. The app gets a clear rhythm: zoom in to one project, zoom out across the portfolio. Two clicks in either direction.

The zoom rhythm is the part users feel, even if they can't articulate why it's easier. From the portfolio overview, two clicks into any view inside a project. From deep inside a project, two clicks back out. Symmetric.

## What happened

Support tickets on navigation questions dropped 32% after launch. Users started reaching for the org-level portfolio views in ways they hadn't before. The zoom-out had existed in the old nav, technically, but nobody used it because there wasn't a clear path back in.

> "The team at Join is very brave for tackling this kind of UX issue."

> "So valuable for end users and their organizations as they will discover and use more of Join's capabilities."

## The remaining argument

The project-first model works for users who manage a handful of projects. Power users managing dozens still want a cross-project view that cuts across the hierarchy — something like "show me all budgets across my portfolio" without zooming into each one. That cross-cutting view is the next structural challenge. I'd also revisit the sub-navigation sequencing. Right now it follows a generic phase model, but different project types have different phase sequences, and the nav doesn't adapt to that yet.
