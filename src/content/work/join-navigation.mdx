## The problem


Join is like Jira for construction. Think Jira, but for the people
who build actual buildings. Construction teams use it to collaborate
around building plans and manage the decisions that move a project
forward.

Join's product surface had grown feature-by-feature as the company
shipped. Each new capability claimed its spot in the navigation, and
over time the labeling drifted away from what users actually thought
about. Two symptoms stacked on each other. Features were buried
behind non-obvious labels, so users couldn't find them even when they
used them every day. And the app had no clear rhythm between
"I'm working inside a specific project" and "show me my whole
portfolio at once." Users got lost between the two views without
knowing how to get back.

The signal came from both sides. Customer success was absorbing
support tickets clustered around "how do I get to X," and usability
sessions confirmed users couldn't describe Join's structure in the
product's own terms. They described it in terms of projects.

## The insight


Early explorations leaned toward a flatter feature-first nav with
better labels. Research showed relabeling wasn't enough. The problem
was that features were top-level at all. The mental-model shift
happened in the third or fourth round of usability testing, when
users kept describing Join in terms of projects no matter what the
nav labels said.

The second half of the story was the rhythm between project-level
work and portfolio-level oversight. Users needed to move fluidly
between "I'm working inside project X" and "show me all my projects
at once" without losing their place. The redesign built a two-click
path in each direction: two clicks from any deep view out to the
full portfolio, two clicks back in to any specific view inside a
project.

## The solution


<FullBleedImage
  src={joinNav02}
  alt="Project-first navigation showing a single project's context with features nested as sub-navigation"
/>

**Projects as the top-level unit.** The nav's top level isn't a list
of features anymore. It's the portfolio, then the specific project
you're working in. Features show up as sub-navigation inside the
project's context, sequenced to match the phase of work the project
is in.

<FullBleedImage
  src={joinNav03}
  alt="The two-click rhythm from portfolio overview into a specific project"
/>

**A two-click zoom rhythm.** From the portfolio overview, two clicks
take you into any view inside a specific project. From deep inside a
project, two clicks take you back out to the portfolio. Symmetric.
The rhythm is the part users feel, even if they couldn't articulate
why the new nav is easier.

<FullBleedImage
  src={joinNav04}
  alt="Feature discovery happening as a side effect of project context rather than through active teaching"
/>

**Feature discovery as a side effect of project context.** When users
land on a project, the nav surfaces the features relevant to that
project's current phase. An estimating project surfaces estimation
features. A pre-construction project surfaces Ideas and Scenarios.
Feature discovery becomes a natural consequence of being in a
project, not a thing the product has to actively teach.

### What I chose not to build

**A universal search or command-k jump-anywhere layer.** Power users
had asked for it, and several Join competitors were starting to ship
it. I deferred it for two reasons.

First, it required indexing infrastructure Join didn't have yet.
Building the infra alongside the nav redesign would have doubled the
scope of the project and pushed the rollout by quarters.

Second and more important: the underlying problem was mental-model,
not findability. A command-k layer would have treated the symptom
without addressing the cause. The symptom was that users couldn't
find things. The cause was that users didn't know what to look for
because the hierarchy was wrong. Shipping the project-first nav
first meant an eventual search layer could inherit a clean mental
model rather than papering over a broken one.

## The outcome


Support tickets on navigation-related questions dropped by 32% after
launch, measured against the customer success team's existing ticket
taxonomy. The ticket signal was the most useful number because it
connected the design change directly to a business outcome: less
time customer success spent pointing users to features they couldn't
find.

Qualitatively, users started reaching for the org-level portfolio
views in ways they hadn't before. The zoom-out had been there in the
old nav, technically, but nobody used it because there wasn't a
clear path back in. Customer quotes captured the reaction:

> "The team at Join is very brave for tackling this kind of UX issue."

> "So valuable for end users and their organizations as they will
> discover and use more of Join's capabilities."

> "Nice. Nav is one of the hardest parts to change in an app. Usually
> there are lots of different opinions from users."

## How the project ran


I'm Head of Design at Join and led this work end-to-end. My team at
the time was 2 full-time product designers, 1 part-time contractor,
and 1 marketing designer. The two junior product designers
contributed to the nav work and used the project as a learning
opportunity to run their own usability research cycles, building on
the research habit we established during Join Ideas.

The nav redesign was the first project at Join where I leaned on
AI-coded prototypes and AI-assisted research synthesis as the
primary communication artifacts. AI-coded prototypes let leadership
click through a real project in the new model and feel the rhythm,
not just look at static mocks. AI-assisted research synthesis let me
cluster usability findings across dozens of sessions faster than
manual synthesis would have, then verify the clusters by re-reading
source material. The combined workflow compressed the communication
timeline without shortening the research.

The hardest part of the project was cross-functional, not craft.
Every feature team at Join owned a surface in the old nav and had
opinions about where their feature should live. Getting alignment
on a project-first model meant asking every team to give up their
top-level spot and trust that project context would surface their
feature when it mattered. Customer success was the advocate that
made the case easier; they were absorbing the support tickets
directly and had the strongest motivation to ship the change.

<details>
  <summary><strong>What I'd do differently</strong></summary>

  **Ship a progressive rollout, not a single switch.** The new nav
  went to all users at the same time, and a few edge-case workflows
  broke in ways that didn't surface until users hit them in the wild.
  Nothing catastrophic, but enough friction that a phased rollout
  would have caught the long tail before it hit support tickets. Next
  time I'd A/B test the new nav on a subset of customers first, let
  the edge cases surface, and then expand.

  **A note on the old "10x feature use" claim.** An earlier version
  of this case study claimed a "10x increase in organizational
  feature use." That figure was directional, not measured with
  rigorous instrumentation, and I'm not comfortable defending the
  multiplier. What I can say honestly: use of the org-level views
  rose sharply after the redesign, and the two-click rhythm was the
  reason. Rigorous instrumentation is something I'd set up earlier in
  the project lifecycle next time so post-launch claims have a clean
  measurement to sit on.

</details>
