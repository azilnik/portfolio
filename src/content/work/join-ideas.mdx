Most software ships empty states and calls them neutral. A blank canvas isn't neutral. It's a decision to make the user do all the work. The best products have opinions baked into the first thing a user sees — defaults that encode expertise, reduce cognitive load, and make the right path the easy path. When a default is good enough to act on, you've compressed judgment into interface. That's the product.

## The evidence

Join is project management for the people who build actual buildings. A less experienced estimator might spec a finish at full cost when a substitute would do the same job for half. That knowledge gap compounds across every line item in every project.

The original plan was a materials marketplace. Customer interviews killed it. Estimators didn't know what alternatives existed, so they'd never browse a catalog. I took on both design and an acting PM role, pivoted the concept to an AI recommendation engine, and we shipped in two months. The key reframe: don't ask users to search. Show them what a senior estimator would already know.

## The craft

**Suggestion carousels as opinionated defaults.** AI-generated alternatives appear ranked by cost-saving potential, grouped by where they sit in the estimate and by construction phase. The ordering is the opinion. A carousel that leads with the highest-value swap is doing the estimator's prioritization work for them. The layout says: start here.

<FullBleedImage
  src={joinIdeas04}
  alt="Three Join Ideas suggestion cards showing alternate materials with dollar amounts and construction classification codes"
/>

**Trust through classification codes.** Each card shows UniFormat and MasterFormat codes. Estimators told us in interviews these were non-negotiable. Without them, a suggestion is just noise. With them, it's a peer recommendation. I used AI-coded prototypes to test card density with real estimate data before engineering built anything — the codes earned their screen real estate.

**Forced comparison, not auto-apply.** Every recommendation has to be weighed against alternatives before an estimator can accept it. The model suggests. The estimator decides. Nothing gets auto-applied. A good default respects the user's authority. It does the thinking; it doesn't make the call.

## The takeaway

Defaults aren't a shortcut. They're the product's point of view made tangible. A blank screen says "figure it out." A well-chosen default says "here's where to start, based on everything we know." The best AI features don't add complexity — they compress expertise into the starting state.
