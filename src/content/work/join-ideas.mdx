## The problem

<Impact>
  Estimators were losing six figures per project to material substitutions
  they didn't know existed.
</Impact>

Construction estimators lose money they don't know about. A less
experienced estimator specs a finish at full cost. An alternate would
work for half. The gap is institutional knowledge, and it compounds
across every project and every estimator.

Leadership wanted to solve this with a materials marketplace. My job was
to pressure-test the concept before we built anything.

## The insight

<Impact>
  Customer research showed estimators didn't need a marketplace. They
  needed the knowledge gap closed from inside their current workflow.
</Impact>

Estimators don't have time to browse catalogs. They don't know what to
search for in the first place. What they need is a tool that surfaces
the options they'd never thought to look for, directly inside the
workflow they already trust.

<PivotCard
  summary="From a materials marketplace to an LLM recommendation engine"

Three leaders had bought into the marketplace direction, and engineering
was already scoped against it. I made the case to the VP of Partnerships
first because her scope was most affected. Once she moved, the CEO and
Head of Product Development followed. The evidence carried the move, not
the argument.

## The solution

<Impact>
  Three design decisions kept the human as final decision-maker while the
  LLM did the research.
</Impact>

Join Ideas surfaces AI-generated suggestions as contextual carousels,
ranked by cost-saving potential. The interesting design problem isn't
the carousels. It's what to do when the LLM is wrong. These are
decisions that shape real buildings where real people live and work.

### Three decisions, ranked by impact

**1. A human curation layer.** A materials researcher at Join validates
every LLM suggestion before it surfaces to estimators. The LLM is a
first-draft engine, not the source of truth. This was the single most
important decision. It made the system trustworthy on day one, even
while the LLM was imperfect.

**2. Provenance by classification code.** Each card shows UniFormat and
MasterFormat codes, the industry's standard taxonomy. Estimators told
us the codes were non-negotiable for trust. Without them, they wouldn't
take a suggestion seriously.

<FullBleedImage
  src={joinIdeas04}
  alt="Three Join Ideas suggestion cards showing alternate materials with dollar amounts and construction classification codes"
/>

**3. Forced side-by-side cost comparison.** Clicking a suggestion opens
a required comparison view. Every recommendation has to be weighed
against alternatives before an estimator can accept it. The model
suggests. The human decides.

<FullBleedImage
  src={joinIdeas03}
  alt="Detail view of a material suggestion with tradeoffs and a cost comparison chart against alternatives"
/>

Nothing gets auto-applied. The "Add Idea to Project" action sends a
suggestion into the estimate only on explicit approval, and from there
it flows to the owner and into the actual building drawings.

### What I chose not to build

**Supplier integration.** Real-time pricing would have tripled the
engineering timeline. The recommendation engine delivered value without
it. The architecture supports pricing later.

**Peer benchmarking.** Cross-company comparisons raised confidentiality
concerns we weren't ready to solve. I flagged it as a fast-follow once
data governance was in place.

## The outcome

<Impact>
  $500K saved for one customer in month one. Decisions 50% faster across
  the product. Two months from kickoff to launch.
</Impact>

Within 30 days of rollout, one enterprise customer saved $500K on their
first project estimate. Join measured the delta by comparing original
line items against the ones swapped via accepted Ideas suggestions. That
wasn't a prediction. It was money the customer would have spent and
didn't.

Across the product, decision-making time dropped by 50%. Estimators who
previously spent hours researching alternates could now get relevant,
project-specific suggestions in seconds.

## How the project ran

<Impact>
  AI-coded prototypes carried the pivot conversation. Two junior
  designers left the project running research independently.
</Impact>

I built AI-coded prototypes against real LLM responses before engineering
committed resources. Not a mockup. A working thing leadership could try.
That artifact did more for the pivot conversation than any deck.

I also used the project to level up the team. Two junior designers at
Join sat in on interviews and watched findings become design decisions.
By the end, both were running their own research independently.

<details>
  <summary><strong>Research details and longer reflection</strong></summary>

**Methodology.** Intensive interviews with a small cohort of three to
five key customers over two weeks, followed by a beta release to
reference customers to validate the direction before general
availability.

**The curation bottleneck.** The materials researcher who validates
suggestions is a real bottleneck. At 10x the current volume, that role
won't keep up. I sketched an admin interface for curation but
deprioritized it for launch. That was the right call for shipping fast.
The operational cost of curation is the thing that will determine
whether Join Ideas scales.

**The pilot I'd run next time.** The initial rollout gave us strong
adoption signals, but watching the same estimators use Join Ideas
across multiple projects over weeks and months would have sharpened
the view of how the tool affects estimator judgment over time.

</details>
