## Context

Join is a project management platform for construction. Think Jira, but for the
people who build actual buildings. Construction companies, owners, and architects
use it to collaborate around building plans, manage estimates, and make decisions
about materials and methods.

I joined as Head of Design, leading a small team while also doing hands-on IC work.
The company wanted to expand into cost optimization. The original vision was a
materials marketplace where contractors could browse and compare products. Leadership
had conviction around this direction, and engineering was ready to build.

My job was to validate the concept with users and design the experience.

## The challenge

Construction estimators lose money they don't even know they're losing. A less
experienced estimator might spec terrazzo flooring at $2.9M without knowing that
an alternate finish achieves the same result for a fraction of the cost. That
knowledge gap compounds across every project and every estimator in the organization.

The instinct was to solve this with a marketplace: give estimators a catalog of
materials to browse and compare. But there was a problem with that framing that
I needed to pressure-test before the team committed engineering resources to it.

## Research and the pivot

I ran customer interviews and direct observation sessions with estimators over two
weeks. The goal was to validate the marketplace concept and understand how estimators
actually make material decisions today.

What I found reframed the entire product direction.

<Callout label="The research pivot">
  Estimators didn't need a place to browse. They didn't have time for that, and
  they didn't know what to search for in the first place. The real gap was
  institutional knowledge: what materials exist, what substitutes are viable, and
  what other estimators at the company have used before. They needed a tool that
  surfaced what they didn't know to look for, directly inside the workflow they
  already trusted.
</Callout>

I brought these findings to the VP of Product and the CEO. The conversation shifted
from "let's build a marketplace" to "let's build a recommendation engine." Instead
of asking users to browse, we'd push relevant suggestions to them based on what was
already in their project estimate.

This was a significant change in direction. The marketplace approach would have
required building supplier integrations, pricing feeds, and a transactional layer.
The recommendation approach was lighter, faster to ship, and more directly tied to
the user's actual workflow. It also opened the door to LLM-powered intelligence,
which became the core of the product.

## The solution

Join Ideas surfaces AI-generated suggestions as contextual carousels, ranked by
cost-saving potential and scoped to the specific project. The system scans an
estimator's line items and matches them against a knowledge base of materials,
methods, and alternatives curated by a dedicated materials researcher.

<FullBleedImage src={joinIdeas02} alt="Join Ideas browsing page showing suggestion carousels organized by source: items found in the project estimate and items commonly considered at the 75% construction drawing stage" />

Two carousel groupings anchor the experience. "Found in your estimate" shows
suggestions triggered by specific line items in the current project. "Often
considered" surfaces materials that other estimators frequently use at this stage
of construction. Both rank by potential savings, so the highest-value opportunities
surface first.

Each suggestion card shows the material name, the dollar amount at stake in the
project, and the relevant construction classification codes (UniFormat and
MasterFormat). Estimators told us these codes were essential for trust. Without
them, they wouldn't take a suggestion seriously.

<FullBleedImage src={joinIdeas04} alt="Three Join Ideas suggestion cards showing alternate materials with cost data and classification codes" />

Clicking into a suggestion reveals the full detail view: an overview of the
material, its characteristics, advantages and disadvantages, and a cost comparison
against alternatives. The "Add Idea to Project" action converts a suggestion into
a formal line item that flows into the estimate, gets presented to the owner, and
if approved, ends up in the actual building drawings and plans.

<FullBleedImage src={joinIdeas03} alt="Detail view of an idea for Ember-Resistant Vents showing overview, characteristics, advantages, disadvantages, and a cost comparison chart" />

These decisions affect real structures where people live and work. A wrong
recommendation isn't just a UX problem; it's a liability. That constraint shaped
every design decision: the transparency of the AI, the curation layer, the
classification codes, and the explicit approval workflow before anything hits the
building plans.

## What I chose not to build

Two features came up repeatedly in research that I intentionally scoped out of V1.

**Supplier integration.** Estimators wanted real-time pricing from suppliers. This
was the marketplace concept resurfacing. I argued against it for launch because it
would have tripled the engineering timeline and the recommendation engine delivered
value without it. We designed the architecture to support pricing later, but didn't
let it block the first release.

**Peer benchmarking.** Some users wanted to see how their estimates compared to
similar projects at other companies. The data existed, but surfacing cross-company
comparisons raised confidentiality concerns that we weren't ready to solve. I flagged
it as a fast-follow after we had established data governance policies with the
customer success team.

## Impact

Join Ideas cut decision-making time by 50%. Estimators who previously spent hours
researching alternates and chasing down institutional knowledge could now get
relevant, project-specific suggestions in seconds. The time saved wasn't just
convenience; it meant estimators could evaluate more options per project, which
directly translated to better cost outcomes for their clients.

With real money on the line, opacity wasn't an option. I designed an AI transparency
report so users always understand how their project data is used and whether it
contributes to suggestions for others. Trust was a prerequisite, not a feature.

## How I worked

I operated as the sole designer and acted as PM for this project. I ran the research,
synthesized the findings, presented the pivot recommendation to leadership, designed
the full experience, and worked directly with engineering through implementation.

The timeline was aggressive: two months from research kickoff to production launch.
I used AI-coded prototypes to validate interaction patterns against real data before
engineering committed to implementation, which let us move faster without cutting
corners on the design.

## Leadership and collaboration

The biggest leadership challenge wasn't the design itself. It was navigating the
pivot. The company had already committed to a marketplace direction, and I was the
person saying the research pointed somewhere else. I made the case by grounding the
recommendation in user evidence, not personal preference, and by showing that the
new direction was both cheaper to build and closer to the problem users actually
described.

I also used this project to level up the design team. Two junior product designers
were watching how I ran this process. I structured the research so they could observe
interviews, participate in synthesis, and see how findings translated into design
decisions. By the end of the project, both were running their own research initiatives
independently.

## Reflection

If I were doing this again, I'd invest more time in the curation workflow. The
materials researcher who validates suggestions is a bottleneck, and as the suggestion
database grows, that role won't scale. I started sketching an admin interface for
curation but deprioritized it for launch. That's the right call for shipping fast,
but the operational cost of curation is the thing that will determine whether Ideas
works at 10x the current scale.

I'd also run a more structured pilot. The initial rollout gave us strong adoption
signals, but I would have liked a smaller group first where we could do longitudinal
observation: watching the same estimators use Ideas across multiple projects over
time, not just measuring early engagement in the first weeks.
