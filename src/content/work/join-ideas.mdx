The knowledge gap in construction estimation isn't effort. It's exposure. A less experienced estimator specs a finish at full cost because they've never seen the substitute that does the same job at half. That gap compounds across every line item in every project. Join needed to close it.

## The situation

Join is project management for the people who build actual buildings. I lead a small design team and ship product across the platform.

The company wanted a materials marketplace. The thesis was straightforward: give estimators a catalog, let them shop for alternatives, capture the transaction. It's a reasonable business model. It's also wrong.

## The wrong direction

I ran customer interviews before anything got built. Estimators told us the same thing in different words: they don't know what they don't know. You can't browse a catalog of alternatives when you don't know alternatives exist. A marketplace assumes the user already has intent. These users had a knowledge gap, not a shopping list.

The marketplace concept died in research. I took on both design and an acting PM role to pivot the direction.

## The bet

The pivot was an AI recommendation engine that surfaces cost-saving alternatives inside estimates. The model scans an estimate, identifies line items where cheaper substitutes exist, and presents them as contextual suggestions. A first-draft engine, not a source of truth.

<FullBleedImage
  src={joinIdeas02}
  alt="Join Ideas browsing page with two suggestion carousels: items found in the estimate and items commonly considered at this construction phase"

AI-generated suggestions appear as carousels ranked by cost-saving potential. Each carousel groups alternatives by where they appear in the estimate and by construction phase. Estimators see relevant options without searching — because the whole point is that they wouldn't have searched.

Every suggestion passes through a human review layer. These recommendations affect real buildings and real budgets. The AI will be wrong sometimes, so a materials researcher validates every suggestion before it reaches an estimator.

I used AI-coded prototypes to test card density with real estimate data before engineering built anything. Each card shows UniFormat and MasterFormat classification codes. Estimators told us in interviews these were non-negotiable for trust.

<FullBleedImage
  src={joinIdeas04}
  alt="Three Join Ideas suggestion cards showing alternate materials with dollar amounts and construction classification codes"
/>

Every recommendation forces a comparison against alternatives before an estimator can accept it. The model suggests. The estimator decides. Nothing gets auto-applied.

## What happened

One enterprise customer saved $500K on their first project estimate within 30 days of launch. Decision-making time dropped 50% across the product. Two months from research kickoff to ship.

## The remaining argument

The recommendation engine works. The open question is whether it scales beyond materials to labor and equipment alternatives — domains where substitution logic is messier and the validation layer needs domain experts who are harder to hire. I'd also push harder on the feedback loop: right now the model learns from the researcher's edits, but it doesn't learn from what estimators accept or reject. That signal is sitting on the table.
