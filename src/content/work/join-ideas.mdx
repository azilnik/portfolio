Join is like Jira for construction. Think project management, but for the people who build actual buildings. I'm Head of Design, leading a small team and shipping product across the platform.

Construction estimators lose money they don't know about. A less experienced estimator specs a finish at full cost when an alternate material would do the same job for half. That gap is experience, and it compounds across every line item in every project.

Leadership wanted to solve this with a materials marketplace. I ran 3-5 deep customer interviews to validate the direction before we committed engineering resources. Every single estimator told us some version of the same thing: they didn't know what alternatives existed. The gap wasn't time. It was knowledge. Nobody browses a catalog when they don't know what to search for.

I brought the findings to the CEO, VP of Partnerships, and Head of Product Development. The research pointed clearly toward a recommendation engine instead of a marketplace. I took on both the design and an acting PM role for the project and we shipped in two months.

## Designing for trust when the AI is wrong

The core design challenge here is trust. These are suggestions that affect real buildings and real budgets. I had to figure out what to do when the AI is wrong, because it would be wrong.

I initially hoped the model could run with more autonomy. Customers pushed back hard. Estimators told me directly that they wouldn't trust any suggestion without human validation, and they were right to feel that way. The AI's biggest weakness was that it didn't understand the specifics of each project. It would suggest alternatives that made sense generically but not for the actual job at hand.

So I designed the system around a simple principle: the AI proposes, but a human always decides.

The biggest call was adding a human review step. A materials researcher validates every AI suggestion before it reaches an estimator. The model is a first-draft engine, not a source of truth. I used AI-coded prototypes to test how the suggestion cards would look and behave with real estimate data before engineering built anything. That let me figure out how much information each card could hold before it got overwhelming.

Each card also shows UniFormat and MasterFormat classification codes. In interviews, the codes came up as non-negotiable for trust. Without them, they wouldn't take a suggestion seriously. It's a small detail that carries enormous weight in this industry.

On top of that, every recommendation has to be weighed against alternatives before an estimator can accept it. The model suggests. The estimator decides. Nothing gets auto-applied.

## What I chose not to build

Supplier integration and peer benchmarking both came up during research. Neither made the cut for V1. Supplier integration would have required partnerships and data feeds that didn't exist yet. Peer benchmarking needed enough customer data to be meaningful, and we weren't there. Both were good ideas for later. Neither was worth slowing down the launch.

## Results

Within 30 days of launch, one enterprise customer saved $500K on their first project estimate. That wasn't a projection. The customer would have spent that money and didn't, measured through before-and-after comparisons on accepted suggestions in the estimate tool. Decision-making time dropped 50% across the product.

## Reflection

If I ran this again, I'd push harder for better data about real projects earlier in the process. The AI's generic suggestions were the biggest friction point after launch. The review step catches them, but that's a human solving a data problem. Better data would let the system carry more of its own weight.

I'd also rethink how we measure accuracy over time. We built good tracking for cost savings but less for suggestion quality. Knowing which suggestions the review step consistently rejects would have given engineering a clearer signal for improving the AI.
