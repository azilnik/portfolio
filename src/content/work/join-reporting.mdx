Join is project management for the people who build actual buildings. As Head of Design, I lead a small team and ship product across the platform.

Reports are how precon teams earn an owner's trust. The Items Report is the working surface where decisions, costs, and status get filtered into the packet that lands in front of an owner on a Friday. Webcor, Leopardo, and McCarthy were spending 5 to 10 hours a week exporting from Join, then stitching PDFs together in Bluebeam to build one coherent submission. Skilled Preconstruction Managers were reverting to Excel macros. Customers told us the Report Builder was too limited for complex projects and too complicated for non-technical ones.

The reframe was sharp: reports are not a downstream artifact, they're a primitive every Join feature has to plug into.

## Two libraries, two jobs

AG Grid Enterprise drives the on-screen working view — filter, sort, group, pin, resize before anyone exports anything. For the export side I picked @react-pdf/renderer (a React component model with real typographic control) so a designer-built primitive could live in both places without a translation layer. Both consume the same data shape, so what you arrange in the grid is what comes out the other side. The reason this lives in code and not Figma is mundane: Figma can't run AG Grid, and the prototype had to feel like a live product to be worth showing customers.

## Parity across interfaces

The harder design problem was making the PDF feel like Join, not like a generic export. I rebuilt our Scales primitives inside react-pdf — PDFTable, PDFScalesDonutChart, a thin set of pdfTokens — so the PDF could inherit the same type ramp and chart language as the web. The first time I saw a 14-page PDF render with the right donut colors and a filter bar locked to every header, I knew the design-system bet was real. Whatever you arranged in the grid (group-bys, filters, sort order) carried through to the export, which is the moment the PDF stopped being a degraded snapshot of the screen and started feeling as considered as the screen itself.

## The viewing-medium reframe

I started this thinking PDFs would be printed. Letter, A4, 11x17 — I designed page layouts accordingly. Garrett flipped it in a customer call: PDFs are never printed. They're viewed on desktop, often inside 500-page submission packets. Dynamic page sizing emerged as the number one customer ask. The "Generated by Join" footer got cut the same week — customers have strict branding rules and this is their packet, not ours. One conversation reshaped the entire page-size question.

## Results

About 25 PRs over two months, AI-coded against real anonymized customer data. The top 20 validated customer requests landed in the prototype, and we put a fresh build on Drew's desk every Monday — that cadence is what got the cross-functional team aligned on reports as primitives. Risks and Buyout reports now plug into the same standard, and that's the part I'm proudest of: the next person designing a report at Join doesn't start from zero.
