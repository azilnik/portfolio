The same data can answer very different questions. Most smart home automations ask "is it hot?" — a threshold question. Thresholds are slow. By the time the kitchen hits 26 degrees, you've been cooking for 5 to 10 minutes. And on a hot summer day, the kitchen is already at 28 degrees, so the system either fires without a stove or needs seasonal recalibration. The better question is "is it getting hotter?" Same sensor. Different derivative. 10x faster detection.

## The evidence

Gas stoves emit real pollutants. The mitigation is simple: turn on your hood when you cook. Most people forget. I have two small kids. I read too many air quality studies. So I automated my range hood.

<Callout label="The core insight">
  A threshold system asks "is it hot?" Rate-of-change detection asks "is it getting hotter?" The second question catches cooking within 30-60 seconds. Same data, different question.
</Callout>

When you turn on a burner, the air near the stove starts rising in temperature immediately. The derivative of the reading over a rolling two-minute window spikes almost as soon as the pan heats. When you stop cooking, the rate flattens and the hood shuts off. Hot days don't cause false triggers because the temperature is high but not changing fast.

## The craft

**Dual-channel sensing.** The automation watches both temperature and humidity rate of change. Temperature catches most cooking. Humidity catches the boiling-water case, where steam arrives before heat. Two signals, one decision. The kind of redundancy that costs nothing in a 236-line YAML file.

**UI-adjustable thresholds.** Every sensitivity setting is exposed as a dashboard slider. A user can tune behavior without editing YAML. This took the project from "works for me" to "works in someone else's kitchen." The difference between a personal hack and a usable product is always the configuration surface.

**Manual override detection.** Toggle the hood by hand and the automation steps back for 30 minutes. The system helps; it doesn't fight the person holding the pan. Automation that overrides human intent is automation that gets unplugged.

**Safety shutoff.** After two hours of continuous operation, the hood forces off and posts a notification.

## The numbers

Detection in 30-60 seconds, versus 5-10 minutes for threshold systems. Total cost about $85: a Zigbee sensor and a SwitchBot Bot plus Hub Mini. No cloud, no subscriptions, no rewiring. Everything runs on the local Home Assistant instance.

I described the rate-of-change idea to Claude Code and it generated the YAML scaffold. I read every line, pushed back on the over-engineered parts, and deployed. The thresholds needed a human and a stove. I ran real cooking sessions and tuned until false triggers hit near zero. The automation has held up through more than a year of daily cooking. The repo is public under MIT.

## The takeaway

Design problems and engineering problems share a structure: the quality of the answer depends on the quality of the question. Threshold detection asks a bad question and compensates with lag. Rate-of-change detection asks a better question and gets a faster answer from the same hardware. The sensor didn't change. The question did.
