## Why this exists

Gas stoves emit real pollutants like nitrogen dioxide and fine
particulates. The Rocky Mountain Institute's 2020 report documents the
health effects, particularly for children. An Indoor Air journal study
found that self-reported range hood use is much higher than actual use.
The mitigation is supposed to be simple: turn on your hood when you
cook.

The problem is that most people forget. I have two small kids. I read
too many air quality studies. So I automated my range hood.

## The problem with existing solutions

Most smart hood automations watch for a temperature threshold: "when the
kitchen reaches 26 degrees, turn on the hood." Clean logic, short YAML,
two real problems.

It is slow. By the time the kitchen heats up enough to cross the
threshold, you have been cooking for 5 to 10 minutes, breathing exactly
the pollutants the hood was supposed to remove. And on a hot summer day,
the kitchen is already at 28 degrees, so a threshold system either fires
without a stove on or needs seasonal recalibration.

## Research and the pivot

I started with the air quality research, not with Home Assistant forums.
Reading RMI and the Indoor Air study reframed the question. It was
never "at what temperature should the hood turn on." It was "how do
I detect that cooking started, as soon as possible, without waiting
for the room to heat up." That reframe came from the physics, not from
copying an existing automation pattern.

<Callout label="The core insight">
  A threshold system asks "is it hot?" Rate-of-change detection asks "is
  it getting hotter?" The second question catches cooking within 30 to 60
  seconds. The first takes 5 to 10 minutes. Same data, different
  question.
</Callout>

When you turn on a burner, the air near the stove starts rising in
temperature immediately, no matter what the room was at before. The
derivative of the temperature reading over a rolling two-minute window
spikes almost as soon as the pan starts heating. When you stop cooking,
the rate flattens back to zero and the hood shuts off within a few
minutes. Hot days do not cause false triggers because the temperature
is high but not changing fast. Brief spikes from opening the oven door
get smoothed out by the rolling window.

## AI as the prototyping force multiplier

This is a solo side project, built in my own kitchen in evenings and
on weekends. It would not have shipped in the shape it did without
Claude Code.

I described the rate-of-change idea to Claude Code and asked it to
propose a Home Assistant architecture using the built-in derivative
sensor primitives. It generated a YAML scaffold with the derivative
sensors and the state machine for the hood. I read every line, pushed
back on the parts that felt over-engineered, and deployed it.

The thresholds and time windows needed a human and a stove. I ran
real cooking sessions (stir-fries at full flame, slow pasta boils with
an oven door opened mid-cook) and tuned the derivative threshold and
the off-delay until false triggers went to near zero. Claude Code
drafted the documentation. I rewrote the parts that read like
boilerplate.

This is AI as a prototyping force multiplier, not AI as novelty. The
artifact that earned the signal is a 236-line YAML file that has held
up in my kitchen through more than a year of real cooking, not a
screenshot of a chat window.

## What I chose not to build

The decisions about what to leave out shaped the project more than the
ones about what to include.

**No cloud.** Every processing step runs on the Home Assistant instance
in my house. No account, no subscription, no dependency on a vendor's
roadmap. Cooking happens whether the internet is up or not.

**No ML model.** A more sophisticated version could train a classifier
on cooking patterns. The rate-of-change insight made that unnecessary.
A derivative over a rolling window catches cooking with a lower error
rate than a trained model would justify for a one-kitchen problem.

**No custom hardware and no mobile app.** Anyone with a Home Assistant
instance should be able to deploy this without rewiring a kitchen. A
Zigbee sensor mounts near the stove, and a SwitchBot Bot sits on the
hood's existing button and presses it. Configuration lives inside the
dashboard users already know.

## Three decisions that shaped how it feels to use

The automation watches both temperature and humidity rate of change.
Temperature catches most cooking. Humidity catches the boiling-water
case, where steam arrives before significant heat. The rate has to stay
above the threshold for 30 seconds before the hood activates, because
derivatives amplify noise and a single jittery reading should not set
things off.

The decisions that shaped the feel of the thing were about the person
using it, not the detection math.

**UI-adjustable thresholds.** Every sensitivity setting is exposed as a
Home Assistant `input_number` entity, so a user can adjust behavior from
the dashboard without editing YAML. This is the decision that took the
project from "works for me" to "adaptable to someone else's kitchen."

**Manual override detection.** If someone toggles the hood by hand, the
automation detects the user-initiated state change and steps back for
30 minutes. The system exists to help, not to fight the person holding
the pan.

**Safety shutoff.** A hood running forever is a failure mode, not a
feature. After two hours of continuous operation the automation forces
the hood off and posts a persistent notification.

## Hardware

Total cost is about $85, all one-time, no subscriptions. A Zigbee
temperature and humidity sensor runs roughly $15. A SwitchBot Bot plus
Hub Mini runs about $70 and handles the physical button press with no
rewiring. Setup takes 15 minutes once the hardware arrives.

Sensor placement matters more than I expected. It has to catch the
thermal plume rising from the stove, not direct burner heat and not
ambient room temperature. "Too close to the burner" and "too far from
the plume" produce the same bad result in different directions, so the
placement guide has diagrams for both.

## Impact

The automation has been live in my kitchen since early 2025. It
consistently catches cooking within 30 to 60 seconds of a burner coming
on, and shuts the hood off within 2 to 3 minutes of the rate flattening
out. False triggers are near zero. When my partner toggles the hood
manually, the automation stays out of the way. The repo is public under
an MIT license, and I have gotten questions from other Home Assistant
users about adapting the approach for their own hoods.

The honest caveat: this is one kitchen, not a benchmark. The 30-to-60
second number and the 5-to-10 minute threshold baseline were both
measured in my own setup. A different hood, stove, or sensor mounting
height would produce different numbers. The claim is "rate of change is
meaningfully faster than thresholds in the setup this was built for,"
not "here is a published benchmark across all kitchens." I am explicit
about this in the repo, and I want to be explicit about it here too.

## How I worked

Solo project, no team. The relevant signal is how I treated the build
when nobody else was going to check my work.

**Documentation as the second product.** The repo has a sensor placement
guide with diagrams, a tuning guide that walks through a real calibration
session, and a troubleshooting FAQ. I wrote them for someone who has
never used Home Assistant, because a YAML file with no context is
hostile to the next person who opens it.

**Designing for the next person.** The dashboard sliders exist because I wanted
someone else to be able to adapt this without opening a text editor, and the
override and safety behaviors exist because a tool that fights its user or
runs forever is not actually helpful. The whole project is a single drop-in
package file with three entity IDs in a secrets file.

**Open source on GitHub under MIT.** The goal was never to ship a
commercial product. It was to prove the approach works and publish it
so anyone on Home Assistant can adapt it to their own kitchen.

## Reflection

The rate-of-change approach works inside its envelope and has a real
limit outside it. A very slow simmer that runs for an hour with almost
no temperature ramp produces such a slow derivative that it can fall
below the detection threshold. A hybrid that combines rate of change
for fast detection with a long-window absolute change sensor for slow
cooking would cover the edge case. I have sketched it but have not
shipped it yet. Calling it out here is more honest than claiming the
current version solves every case.

I would also add an air quality sensor. A VOC or PM2.5 sensor near the
stove would measure the thing I actually care about instead of using
temperature as a proxy. The same rate-of-change math would apply to
that signal.

What this project taught me that I now apply at work: AI-coded
prototyping collapses the time between an idea and a running artifact.
A weeks-long dive into Home Assistant documentation became a weekend
because Claude Code generated the architecture and I tuned the parts
that only a real stove could answer. It is the same prototyping muscle
I use at Join, where I build AI-coded prototypes to pressure-test an
interaction pattern before engineering commits. The side project is
where I practice it without a deadline on top.
