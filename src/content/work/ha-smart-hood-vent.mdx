Gas stoves are bad for you. Turn the hood on when you cook and you're mostly fine. Nobody remembers. I have two kids and have read too many air quality studies, so I automated my range hood.

The usual approach is a temperature threshold — "kitchen hits 26°C, run the hood." It doesn't work for me. By the time the room actually warms up you've already cooked through ten minutes of fumes, and in summer my kitchen sits at 28°C before I've even opened the fridge. So the thing fires when nobody's cooking, or it needs me to come back twice a year and rejig the number.

I went with rate of change instead. Not "is it hot?" but "is it getting hotter, fast?" Turn on a burner and the air over the stove climbs hard within seconds. The derivative over a two-minute window spikes well before any threshold would. Stop cooking and the curve flattens out, the hood shuts down. A warm afternoon doesn't trip it because hot ≠ getting hotter.

## The product

The automation watches temperature and humidity rate of change. Temperature handles most cooking. Humidity is for the pasta-water case, where steam shows up before heat.

Every threshold is a slider on the dashboard, so a partner or friend can tune it without touching YAML. That's the line between something that works for me and something that works in someone else's kitchen. If you flip the hood by hand, the automation backs off for half an hour — it should help me cook, not fight me. After two hours of continuous run it shuts itself off and pings my phone, in case I left a pot on and walked the kids to the park.

About $85 in parts: a $15 Zigbee sensor, a SwitchBot Bot and Hub Mini for around $70. No cloud account, no subscription, no rewiring the range hood. Everything runs on my local Home Assistant box.

I handed the rate-of-change idea to Claude Code and got back a YAML scaffold. I read every line, ripped out the over-engineered bits, and shipped it. Thresholds need a stove and a person — I cooked dinner a few dozen times and nudged the numbers until false positives were basically gone.

It's been running for over a year. The repo is public under MIT.
