## Why this exists

Gas stoves emit real pollutants: nitrogen dioxide, carbon monoxide, and
particulates. Research from RMI and the Indoor Air journal has documented the
health effects, particularly for children. The mitigation is simple: turn on
your range hood when you cook.

The problem is that most people forget. I have two small kids. I read too many
air quality studies. So I did the obvious thing and automated my range hood.

## The problem with existing solutions

Most smart hood automations use temperature thresholds. The logic is
straightforward: "when the kitchen reaches 26 degrees, turn on the hood."

This approach has two problems.

**It's slow.** By the time the kitchen heats up enough to cross the threshold,
you've been cooking for 5 to 10 minutes, breathing exactly the pollutants the
hood is supposed to remove. And when you stop cooking, residual heat keeps the
hood running for another 10 to 15 minutes after the stove is off.

**It false-triggers.** On a hot summer day, the kitchen might already be at 28
degrees. A threshold-based system either triggers falsely or requires seasonal
recalibration.

## The insight: rate of change

Instead of watching the temperature value, I watch how fast it's changing. This
is the derivative of the temperature reading: degrees per minute over a rolling
two-minute window.

<Callout label="The core insight">
  A threshold-based system asks "is it hot?" Rate-of-change detection asks "is it
  getting hotter?" The second question catches cooking activity within 30 to 60
  seconds. The first takes 5 to 10 minutes. Same sensor, same data, different
  question.
</Callout>

When you turn on a burner, the temperature near the stove starts rising
immediately. The rate of change spikes. The hood turns on within 30 to 60
seconds. When you stop cooking, the rate flattens back to zero. The hood turns
off within 2 to 3 minutes.

Hot days don't cause false triggers because the temperature isn't changing fast;
it's just high. And brief spikes (opening the oven door to check on something)
get smoothed out by the two-minute rolling window.

## How it works

The system runs entirely on Home Assistant with no cloud dependencies. Everything
processes locally.

**Dual-trigger detection.** The automation watches both temperature and humidity
rate of change. Temperature catches most cooking activity, but boiling water
produces steam before significant heat. The humidity trigger catches that case.

**Sustained confirmation.** The rate of change must stay above the threshold for
30 seconds before the hood activates. This prevents a single noisy sensor reading
from triggering the hood.

**Configurable thresholds via UI.** All sensitivity settings are adjustable through
dashboard sliders: temperature rise threshold, humidity rise threshold, fall
threshold, and off-delay duration. No YAML editing required after initial setup.

**Manual override detection.** If someone manually toggles the hood, the
automation detects it (by checking if the state change was user-initiated) and
pauses for 30 minutes so it doesn't fight the person.

**Safety shutoff.** The hood automatically turns off after 2 hours with a
persistent notification, in case something goes wrong or someone forgets to turn
off the stove.

## Hardware

The total cost is about $85, all one-time purchases with no subscriptions. The
setup takes about 15 minutes once the hardware is in hand.

A Zigbee temperature and humidity sensor mounts near the stove (about $15). A
SwitchBot Bot physically presses the hood's existing button, so I didn't need
to rewire anything (about $70 with the hub). If you already have a Zigbee coordinator and a
hardwired hood with a smart switch, the cost drops to about $15 for just the
sensor.

I wrote a hardware guide and a sensor placement guide in the documentation.
Where you mount the sensor matters: it needs to catch the thermal plume rising
from cooking, not direct burner heat or ambient room temperature. I documented
good and bad placement with diagrams.

## The documentation

This project is open source (MIT license) and designed so someone with no Home
Assistant experience could set it up. The documentation includes:

- A step-by-step quick start (4 steps, 15 minutes)
- A hardware shopping list with specific products and prices
- A sensor placement guide with diagrams
- A tuning guide for adjusting sensitivity thresholds
- A dashboard setup guide with a pre-built Lovelace card
- A troubleshooting FAQ

The entire automation is a single 236-line YAML file that drops into Home
Assistant's packages directory. Three entity IDs in a secrets file, one restart,
and it runs.

## How it's performed

The system has been running in my kitchen for months. It consistently catches
cooking activity within 30 to 60 seconds of turning on a burner, and shuts the
hood off within 2 to 3 minutes of stopping. False triggers are near zero; the
two-minute rolling window and 30-second sustained confirmation filter out noise
from opening the oven or ambient temperature shifts. The manual override
detection has worked as intended. When my partner manually turns the hood on or
off, the automation stays out of the way.

The repo is public on GitHub under an MIT license. I've gotten questions from
other Home Assistant users about adapting it for different sensor types and hood
configurations, which tells me the documentation is doing its job.

## Why this matters for design work

This isn't a product shipped to millions of users. It's a side project that
automates a single appliance in my kitchen. But it demonstrates how I think
about problems.

**Starting with research, not solutions.** I started by reading the air quality
research, understanding the health implications, and identifying why existing
solutions didn't work. The rate-of-change insight came from understanding the
physics, not from browsing Home Assistant forums for automation ideas.

**Systems thinking.** The automation handles edge cases that most people wouldn't
consider: hot days causing false triggers, manual overrides fighting the user,
boiling water producing steam before heat, brief oven-door spikes, and the safety
concern of a hood running indefinitely. Each of these is a design decision.

**Designing for the person who comes after you.** The documentation is written
for someone who has never used Home Assistant. Product images of every hardware
component. Mermaid diagrams of the architecture. A tuning guide that walks
through a real calibration session step by step. I treated the documentation as a
product, not an afterthought.

## Reflection

The rate-of-change approach works well, but it's not perfect. Very slow cooking
(a low simmer for an hour) produces such a gradual temperature rise that it can
fall below the detection threshold. A hybrid approach, combining rate of change
for fast detection with a long-window absolute change sensor for slow cooking,
would cover both cases. I've started sketching this but haven't shipped it yet.

I'd also like to add air quality sensor integration. A VOC or PM2.5 sensor near
the stove would provide direct measurement of the thing I actually care about
(pollutant levels), rather than using temperature as a proxy. The rate-of-change
approach would apply to that signal too.
