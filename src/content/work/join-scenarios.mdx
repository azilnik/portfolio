The instinct in enterprise design is to simplify: fewer options, fewer columns, fewer things on screen. But simplification hides information, and hiding information from sophisticated users slows them down. Enterprise users managing multi-million-dollar decisions don't want less. They want everything, organized so they can move through it fast. Density, not simplicity. Organization, not hiding.

## The evidence

Join is project management for the people who build actual buildings. Before Scenarios, the product asked users to decide one item at a time. That didn't match reality. A contractor doesn't decide "which countertop" in isolation. They decide "which finish package for kitchens and bathrooms" against budget and schedule. Forty separate trade-offs, weighed as a set.

I reframed the unit of decision from individual line items to linked packages. Owners weigh whole scenarios against each other. The product needed to match that shape.

## The craft

**Sets of decisions as the unit.** A contractor models a "premium finishes, compressed schedule" option alongside a "standard finishes, stretched schedule" option, and the math propagates across every connected line item automatically. The owner sees two packages, not forty decisions. The structure does the work that meetings used to do.

<FullBleedImage
  src={joinScenarios02}
  alt="Scenarios comparison view showing two packaged project options side-by-side with their cost and schedule trade-offs"
/>

**Flat comparison without ranking.** This was the hardest design call. Sophisticated owners on multi-million-dollar projects don't want to be steered toward a "best" option. They want to weigh the dimensions that matter to them. The comparison is deliberately flat — it surfaces every trade-off without marking any option as preferred. Respecting the user's judgment, not substituting for it.

**Progressive disclosure that serves two audiences.** The default comparison view is lean on purpose. Users click into specific dimensions for detail. The owner-facing read stays calm during a sign-off meeting. The contractor-facing detail stays rich for internal work. Same data, two speeds.

## The numbers

Scenario-decision cycle dropped 50%, measured from creation through owner sign-off. 20% of Scenarios users were architects — a segment Join hadn't been designing for. The comparison view matched how they presented options to their own clients, and they found it on their own.

## The takeaway

When an enterprise product simplifies away complexity, users route around it — into spreadsheets, into meetings, into workarounds. When a product organizes complexity so users can move fast, they stay. The design job isn't reduction. It's compression.
