## The problem


Join is like Jira for construction. Contractors and owners use it
to collaborate on the decisions that move a project forward.

Construction projects live and die on bundles of decisions, not on
individual choices. A contractor doesn't decide "which countertop"
in isolation. They decide "which finish package for kitchen and
bathrooms" against budget and schedule. Owners sign off on bundles
of trade-offs, not single line items. Before Scenarios, Join's tools
asked users to think about decisions one at a time, which didn't
match how decisions actually got made on real jobs.

The symptom was long decision cycles. A scenario that should have
taken a week of back-and-forth between the contractor and the owner
could stretch into two or three weeks because nobody had a clear way
to compare options as a set. Customer success and sales were
surfacing "we need a way to compare options for the owner" requests
from every side of the customer base.

## The insight


The shift happened during customer research. I ran interviews with
the contractors and owners who make construction decisions together,
plus a handful of architects who had used related Join features.
Owners consistently described their decision-making process in
bundles. They weren't asking "which material for this one spot."
They were asking "do I want the premium-finishes package at $X or
the standard-finishes package at $Y, and which timeline matches."
The "package" framing came up again and again.

That reframe changed everything about what Scenarios had to be. If
the unit of decision-making was a bundle, then the tool had to model
bundled options and let the owner pick between packaged alternatives,
not between individual line items.

## The solution


<FullBleedImage
  src={joinScenarios01}
  alt="Join Scenarios overview listing two alternative versions of a construction project"
/>

**Sets of decisions as the unit.** Scenarios operates on bundled
sets of linked choices, not on individual line items. A contractor
can model a "premium finishes, compressed schedule" option alongside
a "standard finishes, stretched schedule" option, and the math
propagates across every connected line item automatically. The
owner sees two packages, not forty decisions.

<FullBleedImage
  src={joinScenarios02}
  alt="Scenarios comparison view showing two packaged project options side-by-side with their cost and schedule trade-offs"
/>

**Tabular comparison without ranking.** This was the hardest design
call in the project. Two scenarios usually differed along several
dimensions at once, most often a mix of cost and schedule trade-offs.
Early iterations ranked options visually, surfacing winners and
losers. Research killed that direction fast. Sophisticated owners on
multi-million-dollar projects didn't want to be steered toward a
"best" option. They wanted to weigh the dimensions that mattered to
them on this specific project, and a ranked UI undermined that
sense of authority.

The final comparison view is deliberately flat. It surfaces every
trade-off side-by-side without marking any option as preferred. The
owner makes the call. The tool presents the options.

**Progressive disclosure of detail.** The default comparison view is
lean on purpose. Users click into specific dimensions to expand into
more detail when they need it. The owner-facing read stays calm
during a sign-off meeting. The contractor-facing detail stays rich
when they're doing the internal work.

### What I chose not to build

**AI-generated scenario recommendations.** There was an obvious
product question early on. Should Join Ideas, our LLM recommendation
engine, auto-propose new Scenarios? Tempting, and it looked
impressive on a roadmap slide. I pushed to keep Ideas and Scenarios
as distinct V1 features with separate mental models.

Scenarios is a human tool for comparing options. Ideas is an AI tool
for surfacing options. Mashing them together in V1 would have
blurred both products and forced users to learn two mental models at
once. I scoped the crossover out so each feature could land clean.
The AI-generated scenarios idea becomes a future extension once
Scenarios itself is well understood, not a V1 requirement.

## The outcome


Product analytics tracked the full cycle from scenario creation
through owner sign-off. The before/after instrumentation showed a
50% reduction in cycle time for projects using Scenarios. The 50%
measurement here is specific and separate from Join Ideas' 50%
number; Ideas measured material-cost savings via the estimate tool,
and Scenarios measures sign-off cycle time via product analytics.
They're different metrics on different parts of the workflow, and
it took disciplined instrumentation to disambiguate them.

The surprise was the architect audience. Product analytics surfaced
that 20% of Scenarios users were architects, a user segment Join
hadn't been designing for. Architects weren't supposed to be in the
target audience, but the comparison view matched how they presented
design options to their clients, and they found the feature on their
own. That discovery reshaped how Join thought about the product's
reach going forward.

Customer quotes captured the read from real projects:

> "This is like a happy meal for decision making. Everything you
> need, all in one place."

> "We used Scenarios on a $150M job and it cut our decision timeline
> in half."

> "For the first time, our owners can actually see the cost impact
> of their choices before committing."

The $150M-project quote is the one that grounded the metric in
reality. A 50% lift on a decision cycle at that scale moved real
money and real schedule risk.

## How the project ran


I'm Head of Design at Join and led design on this project end-to-
end. My team at the time was 2 full-time product designers, 1 part-
time contractor, and 1 marketing designer, plus cross-functional
partners in product and engineering. The two junior product
designers contributed to research and interaction design and used
Scenarios as a learning opportunity to run their own customer
research cycles.

The AI fluency angle on this project was visual density validation.
Early iterations of the comparison grid packed every dimension into
the view at once. Density overwhelmed owners, which testing made
clear. I built AI-coded prototypes of the comparison grid running
against real Join data to A/B-test density patterns in code before
engineering committed to any version. The ability to pressure-test
"how much information is too much" in a working prototype, against
real project data, changed the conversation from "which mock looks
best" to "which prototype works when you actually use it."

<details>
  <summary><strong>What I'd do differently</strong></summary>

  **Land on the right visual density earlier.** Early iterations
  crammed every dimension into the comparison view at once. Testing
  kept pointing toward a leaner layout, but the design work kept
  trying to make the dense version work first. Getting to the final
  lean comparison took more iteration than it should have. Next
  time I'd start lean and add density only when testing showed it
  was actively missed, rather than starting dense and stripping
  away.

  **The disambiguation from Join Ideas.** Scenarios and Ideas both
  claim a 50% decision-time lift. The numbers are real and
  measured, but they're on different parts of the workflow. Ideas'
  50% is on material-cost decisions inside the estimate tool.
  Scenarios' 50% is on the sign-off cycle between contractor and
  owner. In hindsight I'd surface the different methodologies
  earlier in internal conversations so the two features don't echo
  in dashboards or investor updates.

</details>
