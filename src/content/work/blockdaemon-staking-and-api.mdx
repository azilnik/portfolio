## Context

Blockdaemon is like AWS for crypto. The company provides blockchain infrastructure
so that enterprises like Goldman Sachs and Microsoft can build Web3 applications
without running their own nodes and validators. If you've used a crypto product
backed by institutional infrastructure, there's a reasonable chance Blockdaemon
was involved.

I joined as the founding designer. Blockdaemon had acquired my design consultancy,
Gravy, and I came in with the mandate to build design from scratch: establish the
practice, hire the team, and design the core products. I reported to the Head of
Product.

When I started, there was no design system, no research practice, no established
design process. The products were engineer-built interfaces that worked but weren't
designed for the customers Blockdaemon was trying to win: enterprise clients who
needed to trust this infrastructure with real money.

## The challenge

Blockdaemon had two product problems I needed to solve simultaneously.

**The node deployment checkout was unusable.** Deploying a blockchain node
historically required DevOps engineers to manually provision servers, configure
software, select chains and networks, and manage ongoing upgrades. Blockdaemon had
automated much of the backend, but the customer-facing experience still felt like
infrastructure management, not a product. Customers needed support tickets and
engineering help to deploy. Usage was low relative to the platform's capability.

**The staking product didn't exist yet.** Blockdaemon wanted to offer institutional
staking: a way for enterprises to earn yield on their crypto holdings by
participating in blockchain validation. This meant designing wallet connection
flows, multi-chain portfolio management, staking delegation, and reward
visibility. All zero-to-one work in a domain where design patterns were still
being invented.

The company was also preparing for a major funding round. The products I designed
would be part of the pitch to investors.

## Node deployment checkout

The first product I tackled was the node deployment flow. The goal was to turn an
infrastructure provisioning process into something that felt like a purchase: pick
a blockchain, configure your node, enter payment, deploy.

<FullBleedImage src={bdCheckout02} alt="Node deployment page showing blockchain protocol cards for BNB Smart Chain, Ethereum, Solana, Avalanche, Polygon, and Arbitrum with pricing and deploy actions" />

The main design challenge was progressive disclosure. A node deployment involves
dozens of configuration decisions: which blockchain protocol, which network
(mainnet vs testnet), which node type, which client software, hosting provider,
region, CPU, RAM, and disk. Showing all of these at once would overwhelm a
first-time user. Hiding them would frustrate an experienced one.

I designed a two-panel layout. The left panel walks through configuration in
logical groups: first the software decisions (protocol, network, node type, client),
then the server decisions (hosting provider, region, hardware specs). As users make
selections, the right panel builds a real-time summary with a live price and a
single "Subscribe" action.

<FullBleedImage src={bdCheckoutHero} alt="Node deployment checkout showing the two-panel layout: configuration options on the left, real-time order summary with pricing on the right" />

<Callout label="The design principle">
  Infrastructure automation should feel like a purchase, not a provisioning
  request. If a user needs to open a support ticket to deploy a node, the
  product has failed.
</Callout>

I iterated through multiple fidelity levels with the engineering team, starting
with flow diagrams and working up to interactive prototypes. The technical
constraints were real: different blockchains support different configurations,
pricing varies by provider and region, and some combinations are invalid. The
interface needed to handle all of this gracefully without exposing the underlying
rules engine to the user.

## Staking and portfolio management

The second major product was institutional staking. This was zero-to-one: no
existing product, no established design patterns, and a customer base (enterprise
treasury teams) that had very specific requirements around security, auditability,
and multi-chain support.

<FullBleedImage src={bdStaking01} alt="Portfolio dashboard showing total portfolio value of $108M USD, rewards scatter plot, performance line chart, and a protocols table with Ethereum, Tezos, Polkadot, and Algorand holdings" />

The portfolio dashboard gives institutional users a single view across all their
staked assets. The design challenge was density: these users manage portfolios
worth hundreds of millions of dollars across multiple blockchains. They need to
see total portfolio value, rewards earned over time, validator performance, and
individual protocol details all on one screen without it feeling like a
spreadsheet.

<FullBleedImage src={bdStaking02} alt="Dashboard detail cards showing current price chart, validator status donut, total balance in ETH, and rewards scatter plot" />

I designed the data visualizations to serve two reading modes. At a glance, the
summary cards show portfolio health: total value, validator uptime percentage,
and whether rewards are tracking to expectations. On closer inspection, the charts
reveal trends and anomalies that signal when something needs attention.

<FullBleedImage src={bdStaking03} alt="Ubiquity API dashboard showing compute units usage at 88%, daily usage chart over 30 days, and a request log table with method names, HTTP responses, protocols, and timestamps" />

The API product (Ubiquity) was a parallel workstream. Developers interacting with
Blockdaemon's API needed usage monitoring, request logging, and key management. I
designed a developer-facing dashboard that shows compute unit consumption, daily
usage trends, and a filterable request log. This interface drove the 23x usage
increase by making the API accessible and observable for the first time.

## Mobile wallet and WalletConnect

The mobile experience extended the staking product with WalletConnect integration,
which lets users connect external crypto wallets to Blockdaemon. This involved
Web3-native design patterns: wallet authorization flows, multi-chain asset
selection, and transaction confirmation screens.

<FullBleedImage src={bdStaking05} alt="Mobile wallet interface showing portfolio value of $108M USD, protocol list with Ethereum, Cosmos, Polkadot, and Algorand, and a wallet authorization dialog" />

The authorization dialog was a critical trust moment. Users are granting access to
wallets holding real assets. I designed it to be explicit about which wallets would
be connected, mirroring the clarity of OAuth consent screens that users already
understood from Web2 applications.

## What I chose not to build

**A custom design system from day one.** The instinct when joining as founding
designer is to immediately build a design system. I resisted this. With only one
designer (me) and products that didn't exist yet, a design system would have been
premature abstraction. I designed the products first, let patterns emerge
naturally, and then codified the system from what we'd actually built. By the time
I had a team of 7, the system was based on real decisions, not theoretical ones.

**Multi-signature workflows.** Enterprise staking often involves multiple
approvers for transactions. Some customers asked for this early. I scoped it out
of V1 because the wallet connection and single-signer flows needed to be solid
before we added the approval layer. We designed the architecture to support it,
but shipped without it.

## Impact

The node deployment checkout increased usage by 23x. Before the redesign,
customers needed support tickets
and engineering assistance to deploy nodes. After, they could self-serve in
minutes. We measured this by tracking completed deployments per month and
comparing the six months before and after launch. The usage growth was the
clearest signal that the experience had shifted from infrastructure management
to a product people actually wanted to use on their own.

The marketing site overhaul I led in parallel boosted click-through rates by 64%.
This was the first touch most enterprise prospects had with Blockdaemon, and the
original site undersold the product. I redesigned the information architecture
and page layouts to lead with use cases instead of technical specifications.

The products I designed were part of the materials that helped the company raise
its Series C round. The staking dashboard and node deployment checkout were
featured in investor demos as evidence of product maturity and enterprise
readiness.

## Building the team

I started as a team of one. Over 15 months I hired and grew the design org to 7:
three product designers across two pods, two UX researchers, and one marketing
designer.

The hiring challenge was finding designers comfortable with ambiguity. Crypto
infrastructure in 2021 had no playbook. I looked for people who could work from
first principles and weren't thrown off by not having established patterns to
reference.

I also established the design process from scratch: design reviews, research
cadence, cross-pod coordination, and the eventual design system. The two UX
researchers I hired built Blockdaemon's first research practice, which brought
direct customer evidence into product decisions for the first time.

## Reflection

The biggest thing I'd do differently is invest in customer research earlier. I
designed the first version of the checkout flow based largely on my understanding
of the domain and conversations with the engineering team. It shipped and it
worked (23x is hard to argue with), but the staking product benefited from having
researchers on the team who brought direct customer evidence into the design
process. If I'd had that research capacity from the start, the checkout flow
would have been even stronger on V1.

I'd also rethink the pace of team growth. Going from 1 to 7 in 15 months meant
I was simultaneously onboarding new hires, establishing processes, designing
products, and managing up to leadership. Each of those is a full-time job. I
managed it, but I was spread thinner than I'd like. If I were doing it again, I'd
hire a senior designer first to share the IC load before scaling the rest of the
team.
