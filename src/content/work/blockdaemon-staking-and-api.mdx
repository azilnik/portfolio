## The setup

Blockdaemon is like AWS for crypto. Exchanges and institutional custodians use it to deploy blockchain nodes and stake assets without operating their own data centers. The customer tier is Goldman Sachs, Microsoft, that caliber.

I joined as the founding designer after Blockdaemon acquired my consultancy. There was no design organization. I was the design organization. The company raised $207M at a $3.25B valuation during my time there, and the product screens that showed up in investor demos traced back to my team.

The challenge was building two zero-to-one products -- an institutional staking dashboard and a developer API console -- in a space where nobody had established UX patterns. No competitor to crib from, no conventions to lean on. Just a blank Figma file and a handful of treasury managers who agreed to talk to us. The question was what "good" even looks like when the category doesn't exist yet.

## What we tried

My first instinct was to simplify. Strip the dashboard down to the essential metrics, clean typography, lots of whitespace. The usual playbook for a new product with a complex domain. Make it approachable.

That instinct was wrong.

Treasury teams managing hundreds of millions across multiple blockchains didn't want approachable. They wanted density. They'd been managing these portfolios in spreadsheets and Bloomberg terminals. Our "clean and simple" prototypes felt empty to them. Like the tool was hiding something.

## What actually worked

I hired Blockdaemon's first two researchers and stood up the research practice from scratch. Their interviews with treasury teams changed the design direction entirely. What those users wanted was density plus real-time trust signals -- total value, validator performance, chain health, all on one screen. They weren't trying to avoid information. They were trying to see all of it without drowning in it.

## The details that mattered

**Ubiquity, the developer API.** The second product was Blockdaemon's developer-facing API for querying crypto data across chains. I built the dashboard as the first place developers go to debug what their API keys are doing. Developer interviews revealed the highest-value views: usage against rate limits and request-log auditing. Not fancy visualizations. Just the two things developers actually need when something breaks at 2am.

**Building the org while shipping the product.** The harder meta-problem was doing all of this while scaling from 1 designer to 7. Hiring, setting up design reviews with real teeth, establishing a weekly research cadence -- all while two products needed to ship. The lesson that stuck: you can't wait for the team to be built to start shipping. You ship with what you have and build the org around the momentum.

## Where it landed

The staking product shipped into the $207M Series C materials. When the round closed at a $3.25B valuation, the screens investors walked through in those demos traced back to my team. The design org went from me to seven people in 15 months. We set up the research practice, the design review cadence, and the hiring pipeline from nothing.

Two zero-to-one products, a new design organization, and a Series C. Not bad for 15 months.
