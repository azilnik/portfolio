When your users export to CSV so they can filter in a spreadsheet, the product doesn't have a data problem. It has an interaction problem. The data is right there. The product just won't let users cut through it fast enough.

## The situation

Gradle builds developer productivity tools. Large engineering orgs like Netflix and Salesforce use Gradle Enterprise to keep CI/CD pipelines healthy. As lead designer, I owned how those teams diagnosed slow tests and tracked down flaky failures across thousands of daily builds.

Filtering was scattered across a dozen dropdowns, each on a different view. Power users had memorized which combination of dropdowns got them to the slice they needed. Beginners couldn't find the controls at all. The behavioral signal was loud: customers were exporting to CSV and slicing data in spreadsheets. That's a product giving up on its own interaction model.

## The wrong direction

The first concept was a visual query builder with a clickable canvas. Drag filter blocks, connect conditions, build a visual pipeline. It looked impressive in a prototype. In testing, it was too slow for experts who already knew what they wanted and still too opaque for beginners who didn't know what conditions existed. The visual metaphor added a step without adding clarity.

## The bet

Replace every scattered dropdown with a single typed input. Users describe what they're looking for and the omnibar surfaces filter conditions they can stack. One input for both audiences.

A new user types "slow" and the omnibar offers "duration greater than" and "status: failed" as next steps. They learn the filter taxonomy by using it, not by reading documentation. The input teaches.

Most diagnostic queries are AND chains: "slow tests AND failed AND in module X." V1 defaulted to implicit AND and skipped the full boolean builder. Most of the analytical value for a fraction of the interaction cost. We prototyped against Gradle's actual build event data, not fixtures. Edge cases surfaced during prototyping — huge datasets, odd chains where suggestions misfired. Engineering saw the failure modes in a prototype and moved.

The omnibar wasn't just a filter for one screen. The same filter chain drove every downstream view: the scoped task list, the diagnostic detail, the summary aggregations. One pattern, learned once, applied everywhere.

## What happened

Users stopped asking for CSV exports. When a customer who has been living in Excel goes back to a product's native filtering, the interaction is landing.

<FullBleedGif
  src="/videos/gradle-filter-06.gif"
  alt="The omnibar in use, showing typed suggestions and stacked conditions"
  caption="Typed suggestions and stacked filter conditions."
/>

> "We'd love to see the ability to add it to other filter selectors as well."

That quote mattered most. The omnibar stopped being "the new filter on one view" and became "the filter pattern for the product." Customer pull made the cross-team rollout possible. Every feature team eventually adopted it.

## The remaining argument

The implicit-AND simplification covers most diagnostic queries. It doesn't cover the OR case: "show me builds that failed on module X OR module Y." Power users asked for it. I'd add a boolean toggle that appears only after the first filter is stacked, keeping the simple path simple and the power path available. I'd also revisit the suggestion ranking — right now it's alphabetical within each category, but frequency-based ranking would surface the conditions users actually reach for.
