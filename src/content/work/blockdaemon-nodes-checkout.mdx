Infrastructure products default to configuration wizards. Step one, step two, step three, submit. The assumption is that technical users want control over every parameter. The counterargument: DevOps engineers already know how to buy things. They do procurement at their own companies every week. A purchase flow lands without instruction. A configuration wizard needs a tutorial.

## The situation

Blockdaemon is like AWS for crypto. I joined as the founding designer after Blockdaemon acquired my consultancy. The checkout was my first real product.

Deploying a blockchain node meant filing a support ticket and waiting on a sales cycle that ran days to weeks. Every customer burned engineering time. Growth was linear in the sales team's capacity. The company needed self-serve.

## The wrong direction

The original concept was a configuration wizard. Walk the user through chain selection, network parameters, resource sizing, and region — step by step. It tested poorly. Customers associated wizards with forms to fill out, not decisions to make. The wizard framing turned a confident DevOps engineer into a student trying to get the right answer. It added friction where the goal was to remove it.

## The bet

Reframe the entire flow as a purchase. Chain selection, configuration, review, commit. Every screen reads like e-commerce, not a technical form. DevOps engineers are used to procurement flows. Mapping node deployment to that mental model removed the instruction barrier entirely.

Every blockchain has its own configuration shape. The checkout only surfaces fields relevant to the selected chain. Users don't need to learn the full matrix to get a sensible default. Chain-aware progressive disclosure does the work.

## What happened

Usage grew 23x in the first year. DevOps teams went from opening support tickets to deploying in minutes. Enterprise conversations shifted from "how do we get you a node" to "how many nodes and which chains." Blockdaemon's growth stopped being linear in the sales team's capacity.

## The remaining argument

The purchase metaphor works for single-node deployment. The gap is multi-node orchestration — customers deploying fleets of nodes across chains and regions. That use case needs something closer to a cart with bulk configuration, not a single checkout. I'd also push harder on post-purchase: the deployment confirmation drops the user into a status page, but the real value is in the operational dashboard that follows. The handoff between "I just bought this" and "now I'm managing this" was rougher than it should have been in V1.
