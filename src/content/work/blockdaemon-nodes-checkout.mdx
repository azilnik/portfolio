## The setup

Blockdaemon is like AWS for crypto. I joined as the founding designer after Blockdaemon acquired my consultancy. The checkout was my first real product there.

Here's how deploying a blockchain node worked before this project: you file a support ticket. A sales rep contacts you. You go back and forth on requirements. An engineer provisions the node manually. The whole thing takes days to weeks and burns engineering time per customer. Every node is a bespoke engagement.

That's fine when you have twenty enterprise clients. It doesn't scale when you want to be the AWS of crypto. The question was how to make infrastructure provisioning feel like turning on a light switch -- you pick what you want, you pay, it's running. The complexity is real but the experience of buying it shouldn't be.

## What we tried

The original concept was a configuration wizard. Steps, progress bars, helper text explaining what each setting does. It seemed right because the configuration space is genuinely complex -- different chains have different node types, different resource requirements, different network tiers.

We built a prototype and tested it. The wizard felt like a form to fill out, not a decision to make. Users associated it with compliance paperwork, not procurement. And DevOps engineers don't need a wizard to hold their hand. They need to see the options, make a choice, and commit. The wizard was teaching things they already knew while slowing them down.

## What actually worked

The reframe was about the metaphor. We replaced the wizard with a checkout — the same shape DevOps engineers already use to buy AWS or DigitalOcean instances every week. Each screen looks like e-commerce: a chain, a tier, an order summary, a commit button. None of it teaches the user how nodes work; it assumes they already know, and gets out of the way.

Underneath, the shape is four steps — chain, configuration, review, commit — without branching paths or an "advanced options" drawer to hide complexity in. Each chain has its own configuration shape, so the form reshapes itself to the chain you picked: choose Ethereum and you see only Ethereum options, choose Solana and the relevant fields slide in. New customers don't need to learn the full matrix before they can deploy something sensible.

## The details that mattered

**The review step.** In enterprise infrastructure, the person clicking "deploy" is rarely the person paying the bill — procurement needs something they can screenshot and attach to an approval ticket. Before committing, the user sees the cost, the full configuration, and the SLA Blockdaemon will stand behind. A half-filled wizard form doesn't survive that handoff; a clean order summary does.

## Where it landed

Usage grew 23x in the first year. DevOps teams that used to file support tickets were deploying in minutes, and the enterprise conversation shifted away from "how do we get you a node" toward "how many, and which chains." Blockdaemon's growth was no longer bottlenecked by how fast a sales team could provision.

The thing about the purchase metaphor: it made blockchain infrastructure boring. In the best possible way. You don't think about the complexity of spinning up an EC2 instance. You just buy one. That's what we did for nodes.
