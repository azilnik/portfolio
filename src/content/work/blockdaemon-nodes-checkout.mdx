## The problem


Blockdaemon is like AWS for crypto. Companies that need to talk to
blockchains use Blockdaemon instead of running the infrastructure
themselves. Customers are financial institutions on the operational
side of crypto, from banks to custody providers.

Before this project existed, deploying a blockchain node at Blockdaemon
meant filing a support ticket and waiting on a sales cycle. A
Blockdaemon DevOps engineer would then manually provision the server.
Each deployment took days to weeks and consumed internal engineering
time per customer. Customers couldn't self-serve, and the sales motion
was the gate on every new deployment.

I joined Blockdaemon as the founding design hire after they acquired my
consulting company, Gravy. My first real product was the checkout
flow: a self-serve path that let customers deploy nodes in minutes
instead of weeks.

## The insight


Early sketches leaned into a wizard pattern because that's what
engineering assumed a "deployment flow" meant. Research made it clear
wizards were the wrong metaphor. Customers associated wizards with
forms they needed to fill out, not with decisions they needed to make.
The wizard model kept the UX anxious.

Switching to a purchase metaphor changed the tone of every interaction.
The customer wasn't configuring something they didn't understand. They
were committing to a deployment they had reviewed. A cart, a review
step, a confirmation. The framing was familiar enough that users moved
through it without instruction.

The pivot wasn't technical. The backend provisioning system didn't
change. The mental model did, and everything downstream followed.

## The solution


<FullBleedImage
  src={bdCheckout01}
  alt="Blockdaemon Nodes checkout main screen with chain selection and configuration summary"
/>

**1. A purchase metaphor at every step.** Every screen reads like an
e-commerce checkout step, not a configuration wizard. The cart analogy
frames the action as a commitment, not a setup task. DevOps engineers
were the primary audience, and they're used to procurement flows at
their own companies. Mapping node deployment to the same mental model
removed the instruction barrier.

<FullBleedImage
  src={bdCheckout02}
  alt="Chain selection and network tier view inside the Blockdaemon checkout"
/>

**2. Chain-aware progressive disclosure.** Every supported blockchain
has its own configuration shape: chain selection, network tier,
resource sizing, regional defaults. The checkout only surfaces the
fields relevant to the selected chain. Users don't need to learn the
full chain matrix to get a sensible default; the form adapts to what
they've picked.

<FullBleedImage
  src={bdCheckout03}
  alt="Configuration detail view with resource sizing and regional settings"
/>

**3. A review step that earned the commit.** Before committing, the
user sees a clear summary of what they're about to deploy: cost,
configuration, and the SLA Blockdaemon stands behind. This is the
moment where the purchase metaphor pays off. Procurement can sign off
on a review screen in a way they can't sign off on a half-filled form.

### What I chose not to build

**Custom chain / BYO chain support.** Enterprise customers asked for
the ability to deploy arbitrary, unsupported chains through the same
UX. Blockdaemon's reliability guarantees were chain-specific. The team
could only promise uptime for chains that had been operationalized
internally, and supporting arbitrary chains would have broken that
contract.

V1 stayed on supported chains only. We lost at least one enterprise
deal over this decision. I still think it was the right call because
the self-serve flow needed trust to work, and trust leaned on the
reliability guarantee. A different V1 scope would have hurt the 23x
story by delivering a shakier product.

## The outcome


Within the first year after launch, node deployment usage grew 23x
against the pre-checkout baseline. Blockdaemon's DevOps team freed up
from manual provisioning. Customers stopped asking sales when they
could deploy and started deploying on their own.

Qualitatively, the shift was visible in the sales motion itself.
Enterprise conversations moved from "how do we get you a node" to "how
many nodes and which chains." Blockdaemon's growth stopped being
linear in the sales team's capacity.

## How the project ran


This was my first real product at Blockdaemon after the Gravy
acquisition. I owned the work end-to-end, from research through
interaction design into engineering collaboration. The project was
also my introduction to the team I eventually grew to seven across
product design and research.

The leadership story here is the stakeholder alignment. The Head of
Product sponsored the work. The DevOps lead owned the backend
provisioning system and its reliability constraints; any self-serve UX
had to fit what the pipeline could safely do. Sales had to let go of
being the gate for every deployment, which was the hardest part. I
spent more time making the case for giving up that control than I did
on any screen.

On validation: I wrote scripts to analyze usage patterns from beta
customers during the rollout, rather than leaning on intuition about
what the flow was doing. That scripted-analysis habit was the seed of
the AI-coded prototyping workflow I run at Join today. In 2021 I was
hand-rolling data scripts; now I'd do the same validation faster with
AI-coded prototypes against real product data.

<details>
  <summary><strong>What I'd do differently</strong></summary>

  **Push harder on post-deployment monitoring.** The checkout nailed the
  "commit to deploying" moment but customers still had to jump to a
  separate dashboard to see node health after the fact. The hand-off
  from "I just deployed" to "is it working?" was rougher than it needed
  to be. In hindsight, the checkout should have ended with a live
  status view of the deploying node rather than a generic confirmation
  screen. The purchase metaphor would have held just fine. Think of the
  live tracking page you get after placing an order. The post-purchase
  experience would have felt like the same product rather than a
  hand-off to a different one.

  **Stakeholder pattern, captured.** The sales-relinquishing-control
  conversation at Blockdaemon has since shown up on every team I've
  worked on. A new self-serve motion almost always threatens a manual
  motion that someone owns today. I now name that tension early in
  any project that automates an existing human workflow, rather than
  letting it surface as the thing that stalls the launch.

</details>
