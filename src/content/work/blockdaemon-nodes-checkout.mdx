New technology creates pressure to invent new interfaces. That pressure is almost always wrong. When the underlying action has a shape users already recognize — browsing, selecting, configuring, committing — the fastest path to adoption is borrowing a pattern they've already internalized. Novelty in the technology. Familiarity in the interaction. The best interface is one the user already knows how to use.

## The evidence

Blockdaemon is like AWS for crypto. Before Nodes Checkout, deploying a blockchain node meant filing a support ticket and waiting on a sales cycle that ran days to weeks, burning engineering time per customer. The checkout collapses that into minutes.

The original concept was a configuration wizard. Customers associated wizards with forms to fill out, not decisions to make. I reframed the flow as a purchase — chain selection, configuration, review, commit — and the self-serve pattern landed without instruction. My first real product at Blockdaemon after the company acquired my consultancy.

## The craft

**Purchase metaphor at every step.** Every screen reads like e-commerce, not a configuration wizard. DevOps engineers are used to procurement flows at their own companies. Mapping node deployment to that mental model removed the instruction barrier entirely. No tutorial. No onboarding flow. Just a shape they recognized.

<FullBleedImage
  src={bdCheckout02}
  alt="Chain selection and network tier view inside the Blockdaemon checkout"
/>

**Chain-aware progressive disclosure.** Every blockchain has its own configuration shape. The checkout only surfaces the fields relevant to the selected chain. Users don't need to learn the full matrix to get a sensible default. The interface hides complexity without removing it — different from simplification, which often removes information users need.

<FullBleedImage
  src={bdCheckout03}
  alt="Configuration detail view with resource sizing and regional settings"
/>

**A review step that earned the commit.** Before committing, the user sees cost, configuration, and the SLA Blockdaemon stands behind. Procurement can sign off on a review screen in a way they can't sign off on a half-filled form. The review step isn't friction. It's the moment the product proves it understood the request.

## The numbers

Usage grew 23x in the first year. DevOps teams went from opening support tickets to deploying in minutes. Enterprise conversations shifted from "how do we get you a node" to "how many nodes and which chains." Growth stopped being linear in the sales team's capacity.

## The takeaway

The urge to design something "new" for new technology is a trap. Users don't adopt novel interfaces — they adopt familiar ones applied to new problems. The checkout pattern has thirty years of muscle memory behind it. Borrowing that muscle memory let Blockdaemon scale without scaling support.
